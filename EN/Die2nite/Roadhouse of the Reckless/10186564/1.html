To give validity to the point of leader-removal being important, I shall provide more psychological boringness.

Group development theory explores how a group comes into existence, how members act individually, and how the group as a whole acts. Specifically, the Tuckman stages of development theory applies very readily to Die2Nite. It claims the following stages apply...

Forming – Storming – Norming – Performing

Forming - The individual's behavior is driven by a desire to be accepted by the others, and avoid controversy or conflict. In Die2Nite, we make the welcome thread and everyone is happy.

Storming - The team addresses issues such as what problems they are really supposed to solve and what leadership model they will accept. We argue and fight over ideas to see who's da boss.

Norming - The team manages to have one goal and come to a mutual plan for the team at this stage. Someone wins and (most) everyone agrees

Performing - The team members are now competent, autonomous and able to handle the decision-making process without supervision. A metacoalition of familiar players works well together.

When an awesome player comes into a town, teaches and leads, the forming and storming phases are completed very quickly. The group moves into norming and peforming and the town does well with their inspirational leader. DERP! Leader is ready to join another town, so he kills him or herself!

Now the team doesn't know what to do. Who is in charge? Who will step up with an idea? Chances are, it won't be clear cut. "Who died and made you king?" I was asked once (the answer was Tobias). The team is back in the storming phase, and doesn't perform well. The only difference is that now the horde is attacking in larger and larger numbers. Days 1-5 are easy, and there's plenty of room to argue of who's in charge. But to send a town into organizational chaos on day 7 can be very traumatic!
