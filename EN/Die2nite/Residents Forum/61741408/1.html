The similarities between Grimmwood and D2N are incredible. They admit that D2N was the inspiration for Grimmwood:

"Yes, Die2nite is one of our biggest inspirations, we wanted to step on the same base concept and build our vision on it and offer a modernized and more polished look, using what Unity and Steam offer. The games will grow further appart, as we add more and more of our ideas in Grimmwood."

The above is a quote from "Alex" at https://steamcommunity.com/app/809280...

I can see this being a game that people could enjoy regardless of whether they like random or more organized towns.

I joined a town and someone put in a suggestion for wall defenses which several people approved of via vote but there are people building something else because the resources are already in storage (the bank). It's probably just like people building the Great Pit instead of making/gathering resources for other structures.

There's an in-game forum for communication along with a chat window.

The map is ok but you don't see items in the zone from what I can tell... you do see enemies if known, villagers (number of other players), and points of interest such as places to search for food, wood, etc.

You can see who's currently online and you can view a player's info to see what things they've been doing. There's also a status log window that's similar to the town register in D2N.

You can go out and explore to uncover the map, search for and/or gather resources, kill enemies, craft inside the town, or any combination of those.

There's thirst, hunger, health, sanity, and probably other statuses. You also get 3 random traits when you start a town but can reroll up to 10 times for different traits.

One major difference is there is no 2-hour wait between searches. You can use up your SP (the equivalent of AP) quickly or space it out or save it for near the end of the day. You can also rest and eat food or use a potion to get more SP.

I'll probably play this for a while. I'm sure town durations will get longer as people get used to the game. Grimmwood seems a viable replacement for D2N but the fact that you can't play in a browser tab really sucks.

It's not as advanced as D2N in some areas like structures, and the UI takes some getting used to, but at least the players are slightly more communicative than the silent people that seem to fill up most D2N towns.

There is a thread in Steam forus called "Not a post-mortem"

If I have to list some other things that went wrong (I hope this might be valuable for fellow developers) is:
- We had to release the game too early, not according to our initial plans, but according to the state of the game; we did realize this is bad and I cannot call it a mistake, rather something that we had no choice about due to the lack of funds. We didnt know a lot of the technical issues that release version had, though, else we would have delayed it.
- Even if we could have stabilized the game to 100%, we were at least 20% behind what I personally see as MvP for this game - crucial features like the tutorial module, rich building choice, night attack variety, at least simple event system, maybe the end-game module, maybe a simple phone app and some others, which are very hard to determine as important DLCs or as bare necessities. Again, not exactly a mistake, time is always limited and, in our case, we definitely needed more and had none.
- We didnt have full-time QA all the way in the team and this was crucial for the stability. This was not a choice as well, we just couldnt afford full time QA from the beginning and I see this as a very important position to have in-house, not outsourced, especially in Agile game development.
- The price of the game is extremely low for it to be sustainable. Some people say that it is expensive even now from a buyers standpoint and I am not going to argue with that. The current game market is what it is and that topic is very complicated, I will not go into it. But have in mind this game is developed for 2 years by a professional team and a lot of things outsourced it costs a lot and the current price point is more suitable for a single-developer games. The income had to cover server costs of players, support and continuous development and it is actually lower than or around the subscriptions for even a month in many online games, it is less than a mediocre in-app purchase in free to play game. Much can be debated whether F2P model would have been more successful for us, I think we could have made it with premium if all was well, if we had higher price and the time to polish the game to deserve it, and some cosmetic in apps to allow our fans to support us further we could have done this in time, but we didnt had time for it as well. The fact is that F2P is more successful using dark side techniques and we just didnt want to be there, weve worked in that sector and we wanted to make a game that we love. Making money was never goal number 1 to us and we would never ruin your experience in struggle to reach for your wallets.
- We could have done some UI and UX better.
- We could have popularized this more or better, I think, we didnt find quite effective ways to get players, but we couldnt finish what was needed for good retention as well and with the low cost of the game big budget for advertising wasnt a viable strategy. Yet free popularization could have been better as well, it is extremely hard to do for a hard to explain strange game like ours.
