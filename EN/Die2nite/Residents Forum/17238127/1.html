What is justice?
By Colette

Crime and punishment. Those two words have been closely associated for hundreds of years. It is part of the human mentality and probably one of the fruits of human rationality. The association exists since the concept of justice was born, and exemplified since the times of the Code of Hammurabi, the law of retribution: An eye for an eye. For every bad action a person makes, like thievery or murder, there should a bad consequence for him or her in return. That consequence is what we call punishment.

I had managed to get myself lost in my endless thoughts again. It is amazing how the human brain is capable of ignoring outside stimuli and focus on its own matters. The moment I lost this focus, my ears were again pierced by the shrill screams of that man. Dragged by the hair and by his tattered uniform by an angry mob. A mob formed by our fellow citizens, who were tired of him and his behavior.

The man was called Eric. He was a thin man, quite young despite what his unruly black hair and beard could suggest. A laidback, carefree man of slow movements, who seemed was never in a hurry. But that day, it was different. He shouted like crazy, struggling with all his strength against his captors. Three strong men were needed to subdue him. They beat him, tied him with some improvised ropes and dragged him out of his hovel. The rusty chain hanging from the gallows seemed to dance along with the rhythm of the wind that swept around the desert. Eric was dragged to the town’s plaza, where he saw that the gallows seemed to be waiting for him. He kept shouting, swearing that he was innocent. “They all say they’re innocent in the end”, a woman said to another as Eric was dragged in front of them.

“You all are gonna pay!”

His begging for mercy stopped as they placed the chain around his neck, and his words turned into insults and threatening remarks. “You’re all gonna die!” he shouted. “You’re all gonna burn in Hell…”

“Go ahead and save a place for me in there!” the man next to me yelled sarcastically at him and laughed.

For everyone, that hanging was like a party. Everyone laughed and insulted Eric as the trapdoor was released, leaving him hanging from that rusty chain. The gruesome spectacle was disgusting, as his body continued to move while the life escaped from his body. I could barely believe something like this could happen. For these people, used to live in this desert, used to the smell of blood, familiar to violence and death, this was just a way of releasing their frustration and anger. They didn’t care if Eric had been guilty or not. The evidence was clear. Nevertheless, they needed a scapegoat, hoping all of their problems could be miraculously solved, or hanged in this case, so everything would be fine. Just a few minutes later, they took the lifeless body of Eric. The same three men just dumped him outside of the town’s gates, as if they dumped a bag full of trash. The party was over, and they returned back to their homes, as it was getting dark already. But I had to stay awake and watch over the gates.

I lied on the floor of my tent, as I couldn’t afford a better place to rest at. The bell would ring in just a few hours, signaling that it was time to close the gates. And the time to brace ourselves. These things would crash head-on against the town’s defenses once more.

Having time to be alone with my thoughts was something not many other citizens could afford. For many them, it was all work in the constructions site until sunset, and then they would go back to sleep. The more adventurous citizens would spend the day, and some of them whole night, outside, searching for supplies, and trying to stay in one piece. Either way, none of them could afford to be alone, in the relative safety of the town, to think for a while. That night I was thinking about the events that had just happened. It was the third time. Two persons had been hanged before, and now it was the third time. The third time the crimes had met their punishment.

I continued to think about this punishment. I remembered the old stories I had heard. How supposedly they used to lock people up in places called prisons if they committed a crime. Jemma
We have been trudging in the mist for days. There were five of us. I, Jemma, a petite girl of fourteen years. My sister, Ana, light and sunshine, full of goodness, five years older than I. Our time together has formed a deep bond between us. A bond that I believed was impossible to break.

A dark shape was outlined against the horizon. A town. I sighed in relief. A town meant other people, a place to hide for a few days. We started walking faster toward the town. The mist slowly cleared. Finally, the tall gates were before us. Ana stepped inside, and waved us over.

There were forty camp beds with clearly marked boundaries. I walked over to the one that was labeled with my name, and put the supplied bag of food into my rucksack. I opened my Citizen's Welcome Pack, and found a radio. Useless. I left it where it was.

We headed over to the well and had just gotten a water ration each when a shout made us turn around. Other people had come into the town. A lot of people at once. I was filled with a strange sense of foreboding as they faced us.

We had unconsciously formed a closed semi-circle, a defensive formation used in the presence of a horde of zombies. But when the leader stepped forward, I knew that this group of people was much worse than the zombies.

The leader was a tall, strong man, with a face as rough as an axe and almost as ugly. It was a face built to evoke fear, a face full of sharp planes and dark shadows. I knew this face. It belonged to a man named Ethan. I thought of what had happened about six years before and was filled with a rush of anger. I glanced at Ana. A battle of emotions was playing across her face.

"Oh, it's you," Ana said at last, tonelessly. I looked to my left and saw that Jim was giving him a murderous glare. Lizzie was scanning the crowd for some reason, but when we made eye contact she abruptly looked away. This day is getting off to a great start, I thought sarcastically.
Out of all of us, only Travis seemed unaffected. He was confusedly looking between Ana and the man, trying to read the nonverbal conversation they seemed to be having.
Oh, how I hated that man. I hated Ethan. He had tried to kill my sister...

I was interrupted from my thoughts when Ethan laughed humorlessly. "Oh, it's you, Ana," he said, smirking. "Long time no see. And look who's over there? Jim. And little Jemma. I've missed you, little girl."
My vision flashed with red. I wanted to jump over and strangle that man. But a loud gasp from Travis stopped me. I looked over. Travis's eyes were open wide, staring at a small, skinny boy who was standing near Ethan. It was obvious that he recognized him. Travis did a series of complex hand gestures, which the skinny boy returned.
Next to me, I felt Lizzie stiffen, then nod slowly.

Ana broke the tense silence by speaking. "I can see that you've gathered your meta here, Ethan," she said, enunciating each word carefully. "We'd better leave." She shot a pointed look at a lithe, dark haired woman standing next to Ethan. "I wouldn't want to waste Evelyn's precious time, of course. We'll leave and won't ever bother you again." She turned to us. "We're not welcome here. Let's go."

Six years ago... I was only eight years old then, same as Lizzie is now. That fateful town was only just faint sounds and colors in my memory. But what I could remember was clear. Ethan tried to meat locker Ana. I had grabbed her and ran off into the World Beyond. Jim had come with us. We lost contact with the meta, and haven't seen any of them until now.

I looked back toward Ethan. He was standing in that annoying cocky way of his. I recalled that I used to idolize him. He had seemed a fearless leader, someone who always knew what to do. Of course, all of that ended when he tried to kill my sister.
"Leave, stay, I don't care," he said. "I'd rather you stay, but Jemma has never listened to my words. Right, Jemma?" His mouth formed a predatory grin.
I didn't bother to grace this with a reply. My mind was whirring. To meet these people here... I don't even know what will happen in the next few days. An assassination attempt? A peace treaty? I sighed.

Suddenly, the dark-haired woman - Evelyn - spoke up. "They can't stay here," she said resolutely. "She can't stay here." Evelyn nodded toward Ana. I recalled that even back when we were in the meta, Ana and Evelyn had this weird vibe between them. I suddenly realized something: Ana was acting strangely. Instead of the light-hearted, playful girl she usually was, today she seemed almost sinister. I tried to shake off the feeling but couldn't.
