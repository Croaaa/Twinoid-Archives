They gathered in the center of town waiting for her. Colette walked through the group of approximately 40 and stepped up on the makeshift podium of pallets and plywood. She looked over the sea of faces raised to her and tried to make brief eye contact with as many as she could. She cleared her throat. Her voice was gravelly.
"Last night we were attacked again. The walls held. Today we will walk the perimeter on the outside and fortify them again. Every night the horde grows bigger. Tonight we can expect a significant breech."
A murmur of fearful acknowledgment swept through the crowd.
Colette paused until all eyes were on her again.
She thought, they all thought, that coming to this place, this abandoned town and building a fortress would deter the horde. That they would be left alone. But since the first sentry had made his way to the walls the horde continued to multiple each night.

Their only tools were makeshift weapons. Heavy appliances, no longer functional in this post apocalyptic world. Daggers and machetes, and other weapons only useful for close combat.

"Everyone will need to be on watch again tonight"
"What about the prisoners?" Someone asked from the crowd.

Colette knew it was a fair question. Every night if any of the hordes made it through the gates or over the walls, they were taken prisoner. They could be used as bargaining chips with the horde. Sometimes they were freed if the horde left. Sometimes they were freed with notes to let the horde know they weren't cruel monsters. That they would fight if it became a necessity. Only wanting to be left alone.

The townspeople referred to their enclosure as "the zoo".

And the zoo was filled with small tents and fire pits. For the prisoners comfort. Everyday they were provided with meat. Not human meat. But any domesticated animals or strays they found. The townspeople shared with the prisoners. And, of course, any vegetables they managed to scrap up.

Because the prisoners were treated decently and fed and watered they grew more comfortable in their zoo. Sometimes you could even hear low laughter from among them. That was cut off abruptly when a guard turned towards them.

Colette knew the prisoners listened to the morning meetings.
So her answer to “what about the prisoners?” was coached for those that listened as well as to her own people.
"I think the time is almost near to use them as a barter"


That afternoon the daily meat that was given to the prisoners was laced with N677 and a slight sedative. When they had finished their afternoon meal most of the prisoners were sleeping. A few realizing what was happening roared in anger. Or helplessness. It was easy to then move among them all and give them a shot of N800.

The dose in the meat, N677 was the vaccine/virus that was given to
most of the population before the apocalypse. Touted as the new vaccine to combat a virulent strain of deadly flu. It was made mandatory for the whole country to have the shot.

When the "Flu" symptoms appeared anyway the hospitals and clinics were inundated with people thinking they were dying. The symptoms included vision changes, a sharp increase in night vision, decreased heart rate in spite of dangerously high temperatures, increased thirst and on their torso the appearance of big carbuncle type sores, only seen in late stages with HIV patients.

The cure? Another government mandated drug endorsed and distributed by the Center For Communicable Disease Control, N800. Most people took the N800.
Except the groups of people that sweated the N677 vaccine/virus out and survived. These were people that usually refused vaccines and most were vegetarians or Vegans. Most had figured out the "cure" was worst than the disease.
Like addicts surfacing after withdrawals. They survived.

Those that trusted the government and convinced by the CCDC took the N800, and within hours became less a person, more a modified mass of tissue and functioning organs. Their heart rate slowed even more. The improved vision stayed. They still had cohesive, coherent thought. As a matter of fact, their brainwaves were extraordinary. Almost all alpha waves. These are calming waves of the brain that most people only have short bursts of. However, most of the survivors were horrified of the needs their body demanded. Such as an overwhelming hunger for meat. And over time learned that any meats satisfied their hunger. Whole families had to learn a different way to survive. A different way to live and love. A different way to measure self control.

Because the people that survived the flu were envied to the point of hate, some of the newly modified humans with diminished mentality in their life before these supposedly inoculations and in this new state of being saw them as food. Food on the hoof. The survivors had to learn to cope. They banded up with other survivors and moved away from urban areas. Some hid, built new towns and handpicked their town members. Like a coalition.
Others, out of fear and ignorance became a group of dedicated hunters. Their motto? residui sumus "we are survivors. Over time, their fanaticism became obsession.

So Colette decided to do the only thing she could. In defense of their new town they needed more soldiers.

Along with several of the towns people, Colette stood at the fence surrounding the zoo. This would be a difficult night.
She waited until they awoke early that evening. The last sleep they would ever need. The sun was slowly descending in the sky. They could see each other clearly though. She waited. Until they looked at each other. Until their faces filled with realization of this new horror. They were paler, the areas below their eyes darker, if they looked closer they would see the skin on their faces seemed to droop a little as if it had lost some muscle tone. She looked over the sea of new faces. Men and women clutching each other. A few scattered children. Making small whimpering sounds. Colette was betting on their will to live....

She said in her gravelly post apocalyptic voice.
"You are now among the hunted. You can stand with us or you can take your chances alone outside the wall. As you must know, there is no cure for "zombies" You have no place to go now. You will be hunted down like the animals people think your are. But you are welcome here. You will be treated with dignity no matter your choice. You are still you. As you have seen while here, we respect each other. And eat food of our choice. We are not animals.

She tried to make eye contact with as many as possible. Looking into their newly glittering eyes. Trying to reassure them without words that this new way of being, of living, was not so terrible. That they no longer had to live a life of a nomad. They were home.
Almost all met her eyes for the first time since being taken prisoner.

Colette unlocked the gate to the zoo and stepped back. Not one of the newly turned made an attempt to leave the town or ran for the gate.. They stepped out of the zoo as free people. A new understanding in their eyes.

Dark The Raven

Odin the great is immortal. His curiosity has led him to travel the world and he has seen sights untold. The beauties of the world that generally goes unnoticed by the mortals that inhabit this wonderful place. Each season Mother Nature in all her glory drapes herself in the jewels of nature. Just looking makes his soul ache. And he has witnessed the devastation man can wreck on not only Gods bounty but on themselves. He has been called by many names. Raven King, Dark Lord....Dark The Raven is the one that seems to stick the longest. Immortality means he usually walks alone. Yet, he has two fellow travelers. To call them "pets" insults both Odin and his companions.
Two large ravens.

When the ravens chose to wander with him, Huginn and Muninn were given several gifts by Odin. The ability to speak and the ability to see as well as Odin at night. Huginn was also given the special gift of thought. The insight to see and understand motivations. Muninn's gift was the ability of mind. The insight to see the ramifications of things that could and could not be changed. At night both ravens leave Odin and roam the world. This in itself was another gift given the 2 ravens. Birds have notoriously bad night vision but Odin’s gift gave them a whole new world to explore. Huginn and Muninn soared and rode the wild wind currents. It satisfied their souls.

A lot of ravens in the 13th century had simple needs, Survival, food, mating. Some aspired to be picked to join the elite death squads. If accepted they would be scouts and seekers, and like the other creatures that were part of the Death Squads. Immortal.
These were men/demons/creatures wearing black hooded cloaks, riding soot black horses with huge black dogs loping beside them.

They rove in packs of 12. Their hoods covered their heads and kept their faces hidden in deep shadow.

Their intentions were to punish the wicked. Abolish the unnatural.

They rode almost without sound. Only the creak of the saddles, the hooves of the horses. The dogs did not bay until a prey was in sight. The horses did not whinny or snort. They all knew their missions were to meet and conquer death.

To see a Death Squad riding meant death was near. Rare was the man or woman that lived to tell of seeing one ride by. In years past people knew that if they heard a squad close by they were to lie down and cover their ears. They squeezed their eyes closed and fervently whispered prayers. Any prayers would do. As long as the litany drowned out the creaks of saddles and the light pounding of hooves. To peek was to draw their attention. And simple people that wanted to live simple lives hid their faces so they could live to tell the tale of how close death had touched them.

After a time the tales of the Death Squads became lore. Generations passed and they were not seen. Or rather, there was no one that lived to tell the tale of seeing them.

Odin, The Dark Raven, was in a town he stumbled across a week before. He had just arrived in the country and was intrigued when he heard of the uprising of the undead.
It was a desert shantytown with heavily fortified gates. During this era Odin dressed as a gunslinger. His pistols low and tied to his thighs. His pockets full of precious bullets. It amused him to play at being human. Even an out of date one. The townspeople although leery of him, welcomed him in. He listened with interest to the stories of fighting for their lives from these "zombies" His companions; Huginn and Muninn remained perched close by. Heads cocked and listening. After a light meal of stew, Odin settled down. He asked his companions to scout that night to see how bad it really was. Assuming the townspeople had exaggerated quite a bit.

At about 1 am he awoke to the sounds of shuffling and moaning on the other side of the wall. Although fear was foreign to him, the sound gave him almost a visceral feeling. He needed to see the creatures that made such hungry sounds.

He scaled the wall and looked down at the mass of rotting corpses before him, 1000s of them!
He had heard of similar creatures when he was a young God. The undead had killed millions. It crossed continents. It was genocide for some races. But the Death Squads had rode at night to abolish the hordes! He thought this might equal that hellish time. The flutter of wings beside him was a relief. He climbed back down to ground level and leaning his back against the wall listened as his companions said the hordes outside the walls were everywhere. Surrounding all camped villagers.

He apologized to his bird friends but requested another errand of them. He wanted them to call on the Death Squads.

The town was quietly jubilant the next morning. No deaths.

Odin, although new to the town had a suggestion.
He asked the townspeople to remain in their tents that night. He indicated he had asked for help and they needed reassurance that they were not observed. The townspeople looked confused but agreed.

That night at midnight Odin again climbed the wall. His companions again soon settled beside him. They returned with the news they had made contact with 3 death squads. Although not one had verbally agreed to help they would make their decision known.
The Death Squad rarely spoke. And then in only riddles.

The zombies pushed on the barricaded walls. Odin could feel the wall shake. The moaning and animalistic chatter below him took on an almost gleeful tone. Odin began to second-guess his decision to send the townspeople to bed

Huginn cawed. He stared out in to the stillness beyond the mass of inhumanity below the wall on which they clung.

Odin saw the dark shapes racing towards the town. He also saw 2 more sets racing from other directions. The relief he felt that the Death Squads were coming to help these people in the buttressed walls was an almost primitive emotion.

The riders along with the black dogs as big as horses themselves began tearing into the zombies. The Death Squads scimitars rose and fell. It did not seem the undead felt pain. There were no screams or even efforts to defend themselves, which should be an instinctive human thing to do. But yet again, a sign of the zombies inhumanity.

Odin stayed on the wall and bore witness. These were loving breathing people at one time. Someone needed to mourn them, even as they suffered an inhumane death.

Once the last zombie fell. A lone rider rode to the wall Odin perched on. The rider raised his face and Odin saw only a white chin. A gravely voice that sounded as if he had swallowed broken glass spoke in the tongue of the lost language of the ancient Norse German.

"This pestilence that has scoured this country will soon be over. The living will see many more dead before that day. The toll will be heavy. We will ride every night until then"

Odin bowed his head in respect. So did his companions.

The man/creature continued.
"Don't make a mouse of yourself, or else you will be eaten by cats."

Odin frowned slightly. What did he mean?

Odin replied.
"It is too much to expect of a cat that he should sit by the milk and not lap it?"

The reply was even more confusing.
"There are more ways of killing a cat than choking it with cream."

Once again Odin replied
"To catch rats the cats take off their gloves"

The man bowed his hooded head in honor. He turned his horse and rode away with his squad following.

Odin climbed down the wall. Not on the inside but on the outer wall.

He walked over and around the dead. He headed out into the desert.

The next morning the townspeople awoke to the site of the undead bodies. And to the newcomer, Odin, missing.

Thus began another legendary tale of Odin's strength and courage. Killing a 1000 undead single-handedly. Then mysteriously disappearing as he had mysteriously appeared.

Meanwhile the Death Squads rode nightly. Saving mankind when they couldn't save themselves. Until the world was once again cleansed. By abolishing the unnatural creatures man had made.

I call it "rebirth"
The only pain worse than death is rebirth.
Rebirth isn’t even an appropriate name for The Event. You are no babe, there are no children. Only adults eaten alive than mysteriously reconstituted. No one has seen The Event from outside their own eyes.
One moment you are fighting to keep the life you have when the soft teeth finally tear your flesh from your bones, your nerve endings on fire as you panic and fight harder as those teeth multiply and fingers pull at you. They always seem to find your navel, digging into the grip and pulling it wide, another cold finger pulls the other way and you hyperventilate feeling your body stretch and rip, if you're lucky you can’t see. But when was the last time you knew luck?

Mercy


Spacekadt was the only one of her group to make it out of town alive. She had fought the hordes of undead that had swarmed her town but when she saw the last of her friends fall she scaled the wall and ran.
Writing for fun
This was her 4th day of camping alone. She was having trouble sleeping. The ruin she was camped in was open to the night air without a roof. But four walls protected her. There was nothing to fear from the sky. Her potential death would come on foot.

When she did sleep her dreams were filled with nightmarish images of her friends falling under the mass of the walking dead.
She just realized today that she was dreaming in color. She had never really thought of the monochrome dreams she always had before. Only comparing her new violently colorful dreams did she realize it was different.

She heard scraping outside. Boots sliding against the small rocks she had placed as simple alerts of discovery.

She sat with her back gingerly propped against the wall. Knees raised and her hands dangling over them.

This was company she had come to expect. Although she tensed a bit. Her knife remained in her boot.

He walked through the darkness of the ruins to the room she claimed as her own. Her small campfire went unseen outside this small enclosure.

He bowed his head as he entered the door. He was tall.

He smiled at her. Her lips curved in a semblance of a smile.

He propped his walking staff by the door.

He lifted the rucksack dangling from his fingers and swung it from side to side in temptation.

He asked "Hungry?"

She scowled at him. Of course she was hungry. Her stomach felt like a deep hole of hunger.

He busied himself putting a small metal grill with 4 legs over the small fire. He placed 2 small chickens on the grill.

He talked while he prepared dinner for them both.

Did she find anything today? Was the well water still dwindling? Had she seen any walkers? Had any seen her? Has she seen the hotel nearby? Was it still full of the undead? Did she eat the dried noodles he had left her?

She tried to follow all of his questions but her head was so muddled. She had hit her head and scratched her back while scaling the wall the night of the massacre of her town. And the death of her friends.

She answered some questions and ignored others. All the questions she did answer were monosyllabic in reply. A yes or no. And even that took more mental effort than she cared to use.

She thought her depression at her friend’s deaths must be overwhelming to her. Maybe it was survivor’s guilt? Why was she the only one that got away? Why didn't she call for the others to run with her? If she had stayed could she have saved at least one? She felt a hand pick hers up. She met his eyes. His kind eyes. And hers begin to tear.
He said
"Quit thinking. Come and eat"

He had found her in the desert the first night. She was sobbing and almost incoherent with grief. She told him what had happened. He told her he would keep her safe. He brought her here to these ruins. She had assumed he was with another large group. But she never saw him with anyone and didn't ask why.

After that first night she quit speaking. Only yes(s) and no(s).

She picked up her chicken. She hated to complain to him but he was a horrid cook. The chicken was barely warm and still bloody. His looked cooked!
But she was hungry and the chicken WAS delicious.

After eating, she lay down with her head on her pack and just like the nights before listened to him tell stories of a civilization to come. Building from the ashes. She went to sleep to the soothing sound of his voice.

That night the dreams were so much worse. Every detail of her friend’s deaths etched in her mind to play over and over again.

She awoke several times to the feeling of a comforting hand on her hot forehead and his soothing voice. "Shhhhh...It’s almost over"

She hoped the dreams WERE almost over. She knew they were becoming debilitating.
Draining her of energy while she dwelled on the horrors.

She didn't hear him leave that night but she was alone when she woke in the morning.

All the next day she was weak.
She again thought her inability to sleep without the nightmares was catching up with her.

She didn't want to eat that night. Even the smell of the cooked food was nauseating. He didn't try to coax her. Just put the food away.

She lay with her head on her rucksack and watched him. She wondered why he always wore black. Loose black pants and a tunic. Her mind tried to make an association with something she felt she was missing. She never really questioned why he helped her. She would thank him when she was better. She would find a way to repay his kindness. But she was too tired now to think of how.

As she closed her eyes in weariness waiting for another night of being tortured with dreams she felt her shirt being pulled up. She was awake instantly. Her wild eyes met his. And she grabbed his wrists.
He said
"I need to see your back Spacey. The scratches are infected"
She knew he had spread salve on them before, when her fever was raging. She had felt cool comfort.
She let go of his wrists. She rolled to her side while he treated the scratches. When he was done he gently pulled her shirt back into place and for a moment she felt the brief touch of his hand brushing her hair back.
The simple gesture brought tears to her eyes. It seemed she cried at every little human comfort now.

Although he never had before, that night he stayed with her.

The next day was just little blocks of sanity. Her fever raged. Once she was twisting and moaning and her whole body seemed to have needles poking her at random. Her body convulsed. She was in such pain. His hand was again on her forehead and she wanted to tell him something. Something important about the night her friends died. But the fever took her ability to speak. During one brief moment of clarity her eyes sought his. She saw pity in his eyes.

She was so thirsty. But although he gave her sips of water it didn't quench her thirst. Or hunger.

Again she tried to tell him...needed to tell him. The night she ran? She felt the bite of one of the undead as she first scaled the wall. She made herself believe it was a protruding nail on the makeshift wall. Or a jagged piece of wood. But she knew. Oh God, she knew.

She awoke again while her fever raged. And with her eyes locked onto his begged for comfort. Surcease in the midst of an unfathomable nightmare.

Joanne's Story.

Joanne was scavenging alone. Typically she would have buddied up with someone but she had just joined a new town today and wanted to show she was independent and could pull her own weight.

She walked due north so she would easily find her way back to town.

Her last town was devastated and overrun by zombies. She hadn't been in that town long enough to make long lasting friendships. And the few women there were the first to be killed.

The deciding factor on her leaving the rest of the survivors behind was a simple one. Either she chose one of the remaining men as a temporary mate or there would be one forced on her. She had already seen the sidelong glances. And the open leers.

So she snuck out the first chance she got.

She shook off the memories. New place. New chance to make friends.

She looked at the cast of the sun. She thought maybe she had walked a mile. Up ahead she saw a few buildings. Ruins of another town. Underneath the light covering of wind blown sand was blacktop. Yeah!!! She was delighted! Walking on loose and shifting sand was hell on your calf muscles! She had plenty of time to take a quick look at the first building at least.

She saw a signpost ahead. It read "Valley Children Drive"

The building was a single story but sprawling.

Maybe a day care? She saw a parapet on the roof of the building and hoped it was just ornamental and not used as a guard tower.

Most children facilities are built to encourage the child not to fear the building. So they may look one story from the drive up but can actually be four or five stories as they can be built on a slope in the back. This one was built like a castle.

As she neared the building she saw a little girl of about 5 just outside the front doors. When the child saw her she screamed and ran into the propped open doors.

Joanne yelled "Hi! Wait, I'm not going to hurt you!"

But all she heard was the echoing scream as the child ran further on into the building.

Other than blonde curls and a petite tiny frame Joanne didn't get a good look at her. She hoped she was cared for. She could vouch she had remarkable lung capacity!

By this time she reached the front doors. The sign embedded in the grass beside the walkway read Valley Children's Daycare.

Joanne didn't know how much she had dreaded it being a children's hospital until she read the sign.

The electric doors were propped open with an office chair. When the electricity is lost most electric doors are devilish to open. These swung outwards.

She climbed over the chair and took a few steps into a well-lit lobby from all the huge windows. She looked up and saw glass surrounding the cupola at the center of the ceiling. No sentries there! The parapet on the roof was purely ornamental. The place was so open, clean and airy that Joanne almost expected to see a perky young woman pop up from behind the counter and ask her if she needed help.

"Hello" she called. She waited a minute and then repeated her greeting. The silence had weight. As if the building was waiting for a response with her.

Joanne decided she was going to search the building for anything useful to take back to town. She didn't have a way to get a message back to them so she hoped they would trust her to know what she was doing. She thought tomorrow when she returned to town they would be grateful she found a relatively clean building that wasn't ransacked yet. Maybe they could move here? Especially if the small children needed supervision and the comfort of adults.

This was a great place to hole up after an apocalypse. She wondered how many people were here? The exterior walls, if built to code, had to be steel enforced. The windows were all installed high and were probably tempered glass. And being on the outskirts of town was a bonus.

She heard a giggle.

She turned quickly. But didn't see anyone. What a delightful sound. She hoped the adults were just as friendly. Being a daycare, most of the kids using this place had probably been under the age of 5.

She heard another giggle. From a different area.

She decided to lure the giggling children out by ignoring them. She walked through the lobby saw a long hallway decorated with zoo animals painted on the walls. Again it was brightly lit from all the widows. What a charming place!

Joanne walked down the hall. She smelled a strong stench and briefly looked into the first door she came too. It was a bathroom with five stalls. The sinks were lower than typical bathroom. She didn't need to enter the room to know where the smell was coming from. Obviously the loss of electricity affected the flushing of the toilets. She wrinkled her nose and closed the door.
She thought poor little tykes.

The next room she came to was empty of people. It was a playroom with a little kitchenette and tables for doing crafts.
She again called out. She thought being a female; her voice may encourage the people in the building to show themselves. Again she felt the weighted silence of listening ears.

Another giggle wafted down the hallway. That was 3 children so far! The 2 she had heard in the lobby and now another down the hall.

She felt encouraged to keep searching the rooms.

The next 3 rooms she searched held nothing of much interest. She found a pack of chewing gum she pocketed. And a bottle of water! That was a welcome relief. She kept the water also.

Although the small tables and chairs had been moved around randomly in the rooms, she didn't see any other signs of habitation. Such as cots or blanket rolls. Empty food cartons. Nothing. Curious!

The artwork on the walls was charming. The small tables and chairs were delightful. This was probably the nicest place she had searched since the world, as she knew it came to an end.

She called out again. Reassurances. After seeing the environment that these people had been living in she almost felt maternal towards them all.

No answer. Just small feet pattering away from her in a distant hall or room. She heard the opening of a door and the soft pneumatic closing.

She hurried down the hall hoping she could see which door was just closing. No luck.

The light from the windows was turning dusky. She hated to rest here without making the acquaintance of the other people in the building, but she needed to find a bed for the night. In this the last room down this hall she decided to make a bed. She gathered the mats that were stacked in a corner. Probably used for nap times for the little ones.

There was a supply closet in the room and she quickly emptied it of playground equipment, balls, hula-hoops, etc.

And laid the mats down.

In the dying light of the day, She wrote on the dry erase board in the front of the room in large block writing.

"My name is Joanne. I am from the town directly south of here. I need to stay the night. I mean you no harm. I would like to talk to someone in the morning. We may be able to help each other?" She drew happy faces and hearts around her message. Even a peace sign.

She went to the spacious closet and closed the door. She wished she had a way to block the door from opening but it opened outwards. She actually felt safe in the dark warmth of the closet. Safer than she had felt the night before in her new town. She opened her canteen and sipped her stale water. It had been an exhausting day and she had no problems drifting off to sleep.

She awoke in full dark. No light shone beneath the door. She heard again the sound that probably awoke her. The soft slide of a foot. The sound of someone making their way towards the closet she lay in.
She reached blindly for the door handle. To prevent it from being turned. Instead she heard the lock on the closet door click. The outside lock.

Someone had just locked her in!

"Hello? Hello!!?"
She tried to turn the knob. It wouldn't turn at all. Her nest, that had felt so safe before she went to sleep, immediately became an airless vacuum in her mind.

She yelled "Hello! I am not going to hurt you! Please unlock the door!"

Her only reply was a muffled giggle. A hand quickly placed over a mouth.

Hyper alert, her heart pounding, she sat with her back against the wall until dawn came and the thin strip of light illuminated the closet faintly. She could hear the sounds of footsteps. And see shadows of people, a lot of people, passing close to her door. All eerily quiet.

One shadow came close to the door and Joanne just knew the person was pressing their ear to the wood.

She softly said, "Please unlock the door. I won't hurt you. I will just leave and go back to my town"

She heard a sigh and saw the shadow move.

She looked at the thin strip of light under the door and like a child, stuck her fingers under the edge. She felt the pet of another hand. She pulled her hand back in and saw other fingers wiggle under the door.

The decaying smell struck her first. In horror she looked at the small fingers. Just bone on some phalanges, rotting flesh on others.
She screamed.

The child giggled. A happy playful giggle. She wiggled her fingers at Joanne.
The lock on the door was turned with a small snick. And the knob began to turn.

WalkinCrow.

The desert is beautiful. Especially twilight and dawn. Most people see a barren mass of sand, rocks, and cacti. But if they sit and listen they can find the beauty.

Around small puddles of moisture you hear crickets and frog songs. The musical yips and howls of coyotes calling out their names and intentions to other packs. The skittering of lizards and mice. The caws of ravens and crows. The shrieks of hawks and the hoots of owls.
The wind shifting sand. Rustling through the small stunted greenery and in and around holes in the rocks. All the sounds coalescing into a cacophony of desert music.

When the small, makeshift towns are suddenly thrown together in the desert there is only a hesitation in the desert sounds. After all the hammering and hurried noises of the small groups of people building safe places during the day, the night is quiet again and the desert music continues.

Except.
When he walks the darkness.
When the world was populated he would walk the streets at night. His boots the only sound people heard. They would look in the direction of his steps and see nothing but shadows. He has had many names. The Thin Man, The Tall Man. The Shadow. And WalkinCrow.

He walks the desert and there is silence. The wind dies, the animals freeze in fright. The coyotes lick their young so they don’t yip and draw attention to the pack.

The only animals of any kind that greet him are the ravens and the crows. For a brief time while he passes these birds rule the desert. Their caws fill the air. Their wings make their own wind.

He visits the towns. Oh, not in a “Hello, hi! Let me in to break bread with you!”
Not WalkinCrow. He has no need for these apocalypse survivors or their fight to survive on.

No his is a secret quest. He does not need to climb walls or beg an entry. Every door and gate is opened for him. To him.

There is no one that refuses him. Because he is small subconscious voice in their heads. A suggestion. That does not even become a memory. When he wants to enter a ramshackle town he just throws out the suggestion and someone opens the gates for him.
No acknowledgment as he enters.

He walks alone through the towns. As he passes his presence is a chill down the spine. Maybe a quick look over a shoulder. But the only thing they may see is an elongated shadow on a tent wall. Or the sound of fading boot steps. Some may get the feeling that they need to cover the leg they left uncovered in the dark. Or maybe cuddle closer to the person they are sharing a bedroll with.

He is made of darkness. His is the harbinger of doom.

Sometimes he will loosen boards; unhinge a gate, little things that the people that walk the wall may not see the next day. But, oh. What a difference it will make when the horde arrives at midnight. The ease of access. The complacency that is broken!

The grief or consternation of the townsfolk. The finger pointing and the blame.

Why? You ask? “Why not?” He would reply. His is a lonely trek. And he finds his amusement in chaos.

This is a cautionary tale. Check your walls, check your water. Daily. Don’t grow complacent with your makeshift town. Be prepared. This is his legacy to you.

If only someone could tell of his sabotage.

But no one knows him. Knows of him. Just the ravens and crows.

And of course, the desert.

Virg saw the dark shapes racing towards the town. He also saw 2 more sets racing from other directions. The relief he felt that the Death Squads were coming to help these people in the buttressed walls was an almost primitive emotion.

The riders along with the black dogs as big as horses themselves began tearing into the zombies. The Death Squads scimitars rose and fell. It did not seem the undead felt pain. There were no screams or even efforts to defend themselves, which should be an instinctive human thing to do. But yet again, a sign of the zombies inhumanity.

Virg stayed on the wall and bore witness. These were loving breathing people at one time. Someone needed to mourn them, even as they suffered an inhumane death.

Once the last zombie fell. A lone rider rode to the wall Virg perched on. The rider raised his face and Virg saw only a white chin. A gravely voice that sounded as if he had swallowed broken glass spoke in the tongue of the lost language of the ancient Norse German.

"This pestilence that has scoured this country will soon be over. The living will see many more dead before that day. The toll will be heavy. We will ride every night until then"

Virg bowed his head in respect. So did his companions.

The man/creature continued.
"Don't make a mouse of yourself, or else you will be eaten by cats."

Virg frowned slightly. What did he mean?

Virg replied.
"It is too much to expect of a cat that he should sit by the milk and not lap it?"

The reply was even more confusing.
"There are more ways of killing a cat than choking it with cream."

Once again Virg replied
"To catch rats the cats take off their gloves"

The man bowed his hooded head in honor. He turned his horse and rode away with his squad following.

Virg climbed down the wall. Not on the inside but on the outer wall.

He walked over and around the dead. He headed out into the desert.

The next morning the townspeople awoke to the site of the undead bodies. And to the newcomer, Virg, missing.

Thus began another legendary tale of Virg's strength and courage. Killing a 1000 undead single-handedly. Then mysteriously disappearing as he had mysteriously appeared.

Meanwhile the Death Squads rode nightly. Saving mankind when they couldn't save themselves. Until the world was once again cleansed. By abolishing the unnatural creatures man had made.

For UncleSolomon

I ran in the front door and skidded to a sudden stop. Nearly running into Uncle Solo. He reached out and caught my shoulders.
"Slow down young buck!" He laughed.
"Uncle Solo!" I cried. "The council members sent me to tell you to answer your cellphone."
I had never seen the florid faced council member that had grabbed my arm so distressed before.

Uncle Solo, Soloman Jebs, was Mayor of Astorville. Everyone agreed he was the wisest man in the City. Except...he hated phones. Called them too invasive. He liked a messenger to bring him news.

Unfortunately today, everyone was in the council rooms at City Hall and I was grabbed to get Uncle Solo.
Uncle Solo quizzed me about what the problem was but I had no idea.

I asked if I could ride with him the short distance back to the park where I had been playing.
He agreed and away we went.

Once we reached city hall he reminded me to be home for dinner.

I had lived with Uncle Solo my entire life. My parents had died in a car accident and Uncle Solo was my only relative. My life was filled with great stories and fun filled days.

I ran across the street to the park.

Hunh, strange. The friends I had played with before running home were gone.

I hesitated only briefly before running back to City Hall.

I entered the complex to find it seemingly deserted. I lightly made my way to my Uncle's office. Behind his desk there was an old fashioned air vent. The kind with a lattice faceplate. I knew from previous visits that I could hear everything from the main conference room. I stifled a laugh. If Uncle Solo only knew.

There were raised voices in the room. It was hard to distinguish who was who. Except Uncle Solo. He was calm. He had a deep and commanding voice. Yet reassuring too.
"Gentlemen! Calm down. Let's sit."

There was more shuffling and noise. Then chairs being pulled out.

The voices rose again. My Uncle called for quiet and then asked one member to give an accounting.
"Solomon. We have a great problem. One of the children is missing. He was taken from his home last night"

My stomach felt funny. All twisty. I leaned closer to the vent.

Uncle Solo questioned the man.

He went on to say that the child had been put to bed and was missing from his room in the morning. He was only 7 years old.
There were murmurings. "Only 7. Only 7 years old...not 10"

I was 9 years old. Why was 7 so important?

Uncle asked if any parents were missing?
Another strange question.

The man answering the questions said not all had been accounted for. I realized it was the Police of Chief answering the questions.

I was sure the boy would show up for a scolding. Why such a fuss?

Just then there was a tremendous commotion.

I jumped!

The noise in the conference room was jubilant.

A child was crying.

I felt a loosening in my chest. The boy was returned. I wondered who it was. And decided right there I would be nice to him at school so he wouldn't be picked on for going missing. Whoever it was...

There was talk again.

I heard a woman crying. 2 women.

Uncle Solo asked one woman what happened.

Her voice was strident.

She said she needed the boy. That she was sick. That her husband was weak.

Did she need him for chores I thought?

The other mother screamed. An unearthly scream. How dare this woman take her child. She had already used her own child up!

What did that mean? "Up"

Uncle called for quiet. The child kept crying softly.

Uncle told the first woman that she had put them into a bad situation. That now she would have to take the child home with her. He asked the Chief of Police to stand.

The second mother screamed.

"No. She doesn't deserve anything. She stole him"

Uncle said "We all signed a contract a long time ago. None would go without. It is all of our fault the woman and her husband were without. We didn't have a child ready for them"

He continued "We have two options. The first is to give the child to her. Since the child is already traumatized"

Give the child to the first woman? What?

"Or divide the child between you."

I suddenly realized why everyone said my Uncle was so wise. Of course divide the child. He would have 2 sets of parents and be loved twice as much.

There was more talk. Some raised voices and low weeping.

But it was agreed that the child would be divided. The second mother insisted she would not do without.
The child's voice was heard "Mom?"

I won't describe what I heard next. Or mention the vomit I left on the floor. The screams I stifled.
I don't remember the run home. To the only place I felt safe. My room.

But I am waiting for my aged uncle. The man who looks a 1000 years old.

I am hugging my knees.
My bowels loosen when I hear a step on the stairs.
