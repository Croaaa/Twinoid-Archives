Hacksaw
goobIf we can kill zombies with water rations, wouldn't the rain kill all of the zeds outside?

If it ever rained, and, if you'll recall, the Shaman has an ability to "make it rain" which does kill some zombies (just a percent chance he'll successfully make it rain in the zone).

I do know a dead body will eventually dehydrate, and a dead body in water will bloat to some unnatural proportions. This leads to a possible conclusion that suddenly drenching a dehydrated zombie could cause some of it's mass to inflate enough to peel off the bones, leaving the zombie to fall apart. It might not be dead dead but it certainly is immobile and not a threat.

Another possibility is suddenly moistening dry bones can cause them to crack and splinter, also leaving the zombie to fall apart under its own weight and become immobile and not a threat.

Both of these explanations might be a bit out there (especially since bloating takes both time and a lot of water), but so is the idea of constructing a contraption to launch batteries instead of just giving us bullets for guns that already exist in game. How fast would a battery really need to be going to kill a zombie? Can we really believe the launchers are launching batteries that fast? I'd think we'd need at least a CO2 tank or something to act as a type of power source to generate the level of force needed (in whuch case, why battery launchers? Why not marbles in a paintball gun? ).

Many things in this game are not realistic, and this is just one of those which works more off of game mechanics instead of logic. As a video game, using water as both life giving to the players and as a weapon to destroy zombies adds complexity to planning as well as limits the number of items that need to be added to the game. You have to choose between surviving tomorrow by drinking the ration to stave off dehydration, and surviving today by drenching the zombie so you can move through the zones (to scavenge or make it to town / a camp site) or defeat them on watch / with defensive buildings (that require water).
