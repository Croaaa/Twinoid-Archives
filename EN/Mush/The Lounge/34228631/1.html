I'm managing people by AP these days.

I think a lot of productivity problems in businesses these days come from work heroism. There are some skilled people out there, who insist on being heroes. They refuse to marry and have kids before 30 because they want to focus on their career. They work 20 hours a day then get burnt out. They'll underestimate the time needed to accomplish something, because they were running on overdrive the last time they accomplished a task. As a project manager, I find them annoying to deal with - to take a mush analogy, they'll work 22 APs a day for a week, then get burnt out and have only 4 APs/day for the rest of the month and get sick. It's wreaks havoc on schedules.

I like the mush assumption that everyone has a limited amount of AP. These days, I put a limit that everyone can only work 300 minutes a day, a maximum of 800 minutes a week, and dedicate an additional 200 minutes/week to training. After that, they go home or are forced to play games or read books at the office.

This seems absurdly short - 1000 minutes is just 16 hours/week. But things like meetings result in roughly 30 minutes of work over the course of 4 hours (waiting for people, chatting up later, listening to things that don't concern you, etc).

I've been testing it for a couple weeks. The result - everything went back on schedule. People stopped coming late because they stopped sleeping at 3 AM and coming in at 11 AM. It makes project management a hell lot easier because I know exactly how much people can realistically do in that week. They're also motivated to do things early in the morning (when they're fresh) so that they can go home early, and so they don't get uneasy explaining why they can't work 16 hours/week. We've also spotted time waste - like once when people were working only 50 minutes a day because of a long meeting, and learned to cut that out.

The brilliant part is that once people have too much to do, they assign it to someone else. You don't see Kuan Ti watering the plants, flying expeditions, fixing doors, and healing people in the same day, even if it has to be done. But in much of Asia, people don't want to lose face by asking for help. Once there's a hard management limit on how long people can work, they're forced to ask people for help and train the younger ones into doing it.

In Mush, there's little you can do with 12 AP. It's enough for your job, no amount of heroism can get you running all over the ship. The heroes are the team players who can get so much extra work done with a little communication.

I find that every career requires some political intelligence.

Intuition takes practice. There's no practical education out there on how to tell if people are lying to you and plotting to backstab you.

Mush is the perfect environment to practice this. You have your majority of helpful people who want the organization to succeed, your incompetent people and freeloaders who somehow made it into the group, and the traitors. Similar to reality, the traitors spread like a cancer. They're harmless and helpful to the organization early on but past a certain stage, you'll wish you cut them off early.

There's always someone who has very different motives than the rest of the ship. I like how these people try to do both things - faking their jobs, yet get away with their motives.

It's a fast practical course in politics. You might face the silently lurking mush who pretends to be good yet turns people against each other in private. You might face the more aggressive ones who take charge and lead the ship to do what they want. You face the ones who falsify evidence and find accomplices to support them (the smarter ones even create accomplices who are not on their side!)

And then, there's deciding when you want to pull the trigger. I find that IRL most people are very reluctant to fire others until there's absolutely solid evidence. By then, the person has done a great deal of irreversible damage. There's a very high cost to killing someone in Mush, even if it's the right move.


I especially like Chao for this. Without Torturer, he has to rely on hard verbal interrogation to tell the difference between mush and humans. Yet he has to do it tactfully enough to not get killed by people who he has offended. Chao also has a second political objective - he's best positioned to lead a mutiny against an incompetent Jin Su. And sometimes there's a surge in griefers who he's expected to deal with and the newbies who throw noise into the investigation.

But eventually you learn the difference between the tone of a clueless newbie and someone faking it as a newbie. You can tell when someone has communication problems and when they have communication problems and are working against the human team. You learn the right level of psychological pressure to apply to people of different personalities and how to heal it from them to make it look like it's nothing personal. And you when the bad guys sweat and crack from that pressure, you learn how to convince the others that it's more than instinct. You learn to set up traps and investigations to cover yourself in case you're wrong.
