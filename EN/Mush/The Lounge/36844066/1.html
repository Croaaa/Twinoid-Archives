Welcome to a mini-StoryGame. Rules of an SG:

1. Author (in this case, me) writes a mini chapter of a few paragraphs, ending it at a decision point (DP).

2. Readers suggest what the protagist should do at that decision point, and roll a 1d100. They are allowed to support an existing suggestion by simply saying "X's suggestion, 1d100."

3. The highest 1d100 is then written into the next chapter by the author, and repeat from (1).

I'll now begin with a prologue of my brand new SG, a spin-off of Mush! Please let me know if you're interested in playing...if there's enough interest, I'll proceed to Chapter 1.

p.s. feel free to start your own SG.

BURIED - STORYGAME BY SAGITTAERI

PROLOGUE

"Remarkable. Absolutely remarkable!"

Dr Roseline mutters to herself over and over again as she bustled around the outside of the ancient wreckage, too much in reverie to set foot inside the vine-covered fuselage. For an "old" lady in her 40s, she's kinda hot. Of course, her secret on keeping her body trim is simple: work, work and work in the lab, and forgetting that eating is essential for her survival.

The doctor lifts her steel cane and taps it on the metal-looking hull a few times.

Ding, ding!

She leans towards the wreckage, her ears almost-but-not-quite pressing on the metal exterior of the grey fuselage. It's definitely some sort of transportation vessel. Radiometric dating places it about 1.5 billion years old. A stupendously ancient sentient alien transportation vessel.

"Oh, beautiful!" she exclaims suddenly and covers her mouth with her hands in excitement. "Its resonance is completely foreign to me - oh, my lord, to be on the cusp of our first extra-terestial-engineering discovery! We must take a sample of this alloy and examine it immediately! Oh, how absolutely remarkable!"

Yes, she does say that a lot.

"I've seen a child with more maturity than her right now," chuckle a deep baritone voice behind me.

I didn't need to turn around to identify the owner of that stupidly manly voice. "Give her a break, Wes," I reply with a smile. Not that I'd ever let him see it. "Just because you're a paragon of maturity, it doesn't mean you should judge."

He just laughs aloud at my snarky sarcasm. He's not exactly childish. Not all the time, anyway. He's intense as hell when he's on the job, but when he's on a break, you can pretty much count on one or two 'anonymous' pranks all over ground base. For some reason, I'm his favourite target. Maybe he misses having a little brother around or something lame like that. Whatever. Not allowing myself to be easily picked on, I definitely returned the favour a couple of times...although I may have used too much chilli on that last one. His mouth is still a little swollen!

"Liam! Get over here and help me cut it down!" yells Dr Roseline from somewhere behind the wreckage.

Surprised by the sudden request, I stumble a little on my way around to her. "Yes, Dr Roseline!" I could hear the stupid prick chuckling behind me at my (very uncommon, I'll have you know) clumsiness. I gave him the finger before disappearing around the corner of the fuselage.

What the-

"Dr Roseline-Dr Roseline, stop!" I yell without thinking and ran towards her. "Uh, I mean, please get down from that...the debris. With all due respect, ma'am." She's crazy! Why did she climb up that pile of unstable death trap-looking debris?!

Oh.

I followed her gaze. There's some sort of artificial markings on the fuselage. Wow. My own sense of scientific curiosity is fanned ablaze in my chest. She's trying to remove the vines to uncover the rest of the markings. I shake my head, smiling. It's so like her to let curiousity endanger her life constantly. But, who can blame her. If those markings turn out to be symbols, it will be our first real sample of a sentient extra-terrestial language!

"Please let me handle this, Dr Roseline," I request, a lot calmer this time. "Let me do this - I'm your research assistant, after all."

To my relief, she hops down from the death trap, onto much safer grass-covered ground. Her child-like excitement is still very much plastered to her face. "Very well, Liam. I'll start collecting samples of the plants surrounding the wreckage, then. Our green house won't be big enough for all the alien botany we discovered on this planet! Oh! Remarkable!"

Yes, she's right, I thought to myself, as I lifted up a long metal-looking pole from the pile of debris and scrape off the vines with it. This planet is so green and blue. It's so much like Earth, and yet it's nothing like Earth. The air here has perfect atmospheric composition to support Earthly organisms. Moreover, one of our more stupendously interesting finding was that the alien life here has the same carbon-based cellular structures as life on Earth, all the way down to their DNA encoding. And yet, all these flora and fauna are completely alien to us. It's like we have no common ancestors, but yet are made the same way. Even the alien ecology is familiar to us, built around the Darwinistic mechanism of prey and predators.

Predators. Suddenly remembering something, I turn around. "Dr Roseline! Please take Wes with you!"

"Oi!" a baritone voice from the other side of the wreckage bark in mock-anger. "No need for you to tell your boss to tell me how to do my job! I'm a very talented security officer, you know!"

I only laugh in reply. "Well, Wes, so far, I've seen no evidence of-" my breath is suddenly caught in my throat. Then, when I finally find my voice again, I calmly-"OH MY GOD!"

Dr Roseline and Wes appear from around the corner an instant later, staring at me with puzzlement and worry.

"Liam, what's wrong?"

"What happened?"

They both ask me at the same time.

My jaw hung open. My eyes are transfixed at the markings on the fuselage. But I couldn't reply, so I say nothing. I turn and look at them, with a god-knows-what expression on my face.

"LIAM, stop it! You're freaking me out!" yells Wes, in a tone one could almost misidentify as 'concern', if one isn't aware of the prank-filled relationship we share. Not waiting for my response, they both hurry towards me.

Dr Roseline gasps audibly beside me. She must have seen it. Now she, too, is speechless.

Only soldier boy can speak after looking at what I was - and still am - looking at. "Hey you two, I'm not a scientist or anything...but aren't those markings..."

"Yes," I interrupt him, continuing to stare at the alien symbols on the side of the fuselage. "But it can't be."

"It can't be," Dr Rosaline echoes. "But yes."

A long moment of silence passes through. Eventually, Wes sighs. "Okay, I hate to ruin this romantic sciency moment you two have going. But, what does it mean?"

"Irrelevant," breathes Dr Rosaline. "The breakthrough here, is that we can read it. It's an ancient alien wreckage, and an ancient alien language, which predates humankind by a billion years...and yet we can read it. We can read it."

Wes crosses his arms in annoyance. "Okay, yes, I get it, the alien language is in English, which is weird. But, what does it mean? What is a DAEDALUS?"

CHAPTER I - PART A

"Oh my lord, isn't this absolutely remarkable?! Oh, oh, boys, I found an opening! Hurry!"

"Dr Roseline, slow down! Stay behind me and my gun, please."

"Don't be silly, Lieutenant dear. This is a scientific endeavour, not a warzone. Watch your step, everyone. The floor's thoroughly oxidised."

"Oxy-what?"

"She means the floor's completely rusted, Wes. As in, it might crumble beneath your-WHOA-OWCH!!"

"Liam!"

"I'm fine, I'm fine, let my arm go. Wes, hang on, my leg's stuck on something. I can't see a damn thing...where's my damn torch?"

"It fell in that hole, I think. Here, let me...huh? Dr Roseline, where are you going? Doctor, wait, stop! Shit, Liam, wait here, I'll be right back."

"Wes, don't you dare leave-hey! Ahh, fudge."

With that, I'm all alone in the eeriely silent darkness. Wait here? As if. That stupid prick can shove it.

The 1.5 billion years old vessel is almost entirely deteriorated near where we entered from. Shafts of natural light shone through the occasional holes on the vine-covered roofs. However, the deeper we ventured into the fuselage, the darker it became. The first room we were in was large - enough to house a handful of land vehicles, and given the entrance we entered from looked like an outline of a hangar door, perhaps it really was a hangar bay. There were doors leading deeper into the wreckage, but they're all stuck, except for one, which leads into a tiny room with nothing but an elaborate virtual-reality type chair...or what's left of it. Dr Roseline was almost certain that the vine-covered platform in front of it must be an electronic display at some point in the past.

I was studying it when the ground unexpectedly decide to give way to my left foot. Dr Roseline barely noticed my predicament due to her excitement...at least that's what I tell myself, because the other explanation of why she would've simply walked off is that she doesn't think much of my contribution to science!

Stretching myself forward, I stick my right arm into the hole Wes gestured at before. Leaning into it as much as I could, my fingertips manages to brush against the bottom of the hole. It's a little sandy, and I'm pretty sure that cold, smooth surface beneath the rough grains of sand is metallic. I yelp as something suddenly scuttles over my hand! Something's alive in that hole! Fudge, no way, I'm not going to be snacked by some extra-terestial vermin!

My chest thumping, possibly in fear, it took all I have to not snatch my hand back. I take a deep breath and push my body forcefully even closer to the hole until my trapped leg is burning in throbbing pain. "Dammit!" I groan and bite my lip. I'm not waiting to find out what's in store for me, here! I systematically search the bottom of the hole with my hands, and to my relief, my knuckles bumps into a familiar rubber texture. It's my torchlight!

After clumsily knocking it away a few times, I feel the palm-length rubber tube rolling in between my fingers and my thumb. I exhale slowly before finally lifting it out of the hole. My torchlight is officially fine. "Shit yeah!" I exclaim to myself, laughing a little.

Relieved that no part of me is in that creepy hole any longer, I switch it on and shone a beam of light on my trapped leg. I wince. Bright red blood is rolling down my lower left leg, over the navy blue explorer suit material! I somehow managed to stab my own calves with a rusted sheet of purple moss-covered alien metal. Great. Just great. Kerry is going to be majorly pissed at me for damaging her precious explorer suit. With the guidance of the torch in my mouth, I manage to free my foot from the tangle of rubber cords beneath the floor.

It suddenly hits me what I'm looking at: under-the-floor electrical wiring! Reaching in, I move the rubber cables around. The ancient alien's engineering is crazy similar to ours. I don't know why am I surprised...they use the same language as us, for crying out loud. What is the deal with that, anyway? It makes absolutely no sense! According to Kerry just before we entered the wreckage, Daedalus means something like a "good worker", based on a millenia-old Greek mythology.

Hmm?

The rubber cords, too, are covered in purple moss. Whatever species this is, it seems to survive on rusted metal and rubber, in a dark and dry environment. How unusual. Without thinking, I pull out my sample kit and scrape a tiny piece of the moss into a sterile sealed container.

"Liam, your leg!"

I sigh as I reattach my sample kit to the right side of my belt. "Wes."

"Seriously, I can't leave you alone, can I? Not even for a minute! You scientist types are all just as bad!"

"I'm right here, Liutenant dear," Dr Roseline's voice echoes from somewhere behind the soldier. I can't see her due to the darkness.

"No, I'm serious, you two," snaps Wes. "If you can't stay behind me and follow my instructions, I'm dragging you both out, got it?"

Dr Roseline mutters a non-committal yes.

"Liam?"

I give him a small nod. He's gone into his intense mode, now. No sense in trying to argue. "So," I start as I push myself up. "What's in the next room?"

The doctor's petit silhouette walks into view. "A long corridor with a few doors. One of them leads into a room that looks remarkably like a kitchen."

Oww. My left leg hurts a bit. I try not to limp as I move towards the pair. "A mess hall? There's only one reason why a vessel would need one of those!"

"You got it, Liam dear. This starship is made for long voyages!"

Wes gestures us both to follow behind him as he leads the way into said corridor. It's pretty much pitch black in here, now, our three torches being the only sources of light. The amount of vegetation has gone down significantly in this portion of the...

"Starship?" I ask, suddenly noticing Dr Roseline's change in wording.

"Yes, dear. The shape of the fuselage is inefficient for sea travel, and is terribly non-aerodynamic. And yet, these corridors are built with practicality and efficiency in mind, so it only follows that this vessel was once used for space travel."

I try to swallow my excitement. Spacefaring aliens 1.5 billion years ago? "Do you think...you know, since they speak English, that maybe they came to Earth at some point?"

"Perhaps, dear. But, if you're thinking that these extra-terrestials are our ancestors, I'd have to reconsider your position as my research assistant."

I blink. "What-"

"It's common knowledge that the English language was first spoken in early medieval England...not any earlier."

That's so not common knowledge! But I bite my lip before I can say anything.

"Whoever built this ship, they must have learnt our language after its inception about a millenia ago. Additionally, the reference to the ancient three-millenia old Greek mythology can only mean that this English is definitely our English."

"But the ship's dated to be-"

"A billion years old, I know, dear."

Creepy. What am I supposed to make of this? At Dr Roseline's request, Wes leads us into another hallway, connected to the one we were in before. The air's getting thin over here, and the metallic interior are not as thoroughly rusted. Shining my torch around, I'm getting the idea of how the ship's meant to look like before nature chipped away at it for a billion years. To be honest, it's in surprisingly good condition considering its age.

CHAPTER I - PART B

Zap! Zap!

Sudden flashes of bright red light fills the room as Wes fires his gun twice without warning!

"Wes, what are you doing?!" I yell at him, bewildered.

"Something moved over there!"

"Where?"

"There!"

"I don't see anything-"

"Seriously-turn your head that way!"

A round, floating object moves briefly into the beam of my torch, before disappearing out of it. Taken by surprise, I swing my torch around wildly, trying to find it again. "Wes, where is it now?"

"I can't see it. Dammit, what was that? Was that an alien?!"

"No," Dr Roseline joins in. "I only had a brief look, but whatever it was, it wasn't sentient."

"What does it matter!" the soldier shouts. "It's alive!"

"Actually," I say slowly. "I don't think it is..."

Wes snorts. "Right, and you know this from glancing at it for a whole half second."

"No, I know this because I'm looking at it right now."

As soon as I said that, my two companions combine the light from their torches with mine. Like a dear caught in headlights, the mysterious object stops moving. It's round. It's metallic. It's floating. And, it has a red glowing orb which looks suspiciously like an eye, staring at us, as if evaluating us.

"A drone?" asks the soldier.

"Yes, Lieutenant. Oh my, and I do believe I was wrong," answers Dr Roseline.

What did she mean? Noticing something, I shine my torch at the wall behind the drone-like object. The entire metallic wall of this section of the hallway has been half-stripped away, but not by decay. It looks like it was purposefully cut out by someone, in perfect triangular-shaped pieces the size of 11-inch pizza slices.

"Remarkable. Oh my. This drone is sentient. Oh, how remarkable!"

What? I focus my torch on the drone again. The red orb briefly flickers in my direction. Fudge. It really is an eye? Is it...evaluating me, now? It flickers towards the soldier's hand, and back at me. And then it did it again. Suddenly, I understand it's trying to tell me. "Uh, Wes, put the gun down."

"What? No."

"Wes, come on."

"Are you crazy, it might be armed!"

Stupid prick. Slowly, I step in between Wes and the drone, keeping my front to it. "It's okay, he's a bit thick-headed and insensitive, but he won't shoot you, promise," I tell it. Suddenly, I wonder if I'm crazy for trying to reassure an alien drone.

Almost immediately, the drone turns around and flies down the corridor!

"Shit!" I quickly run after it, despite Wes cursing at me from behind.

It didn't go very far, however. It's now waiting by one of the many doors in the middle of the corridor. As soon as we arrive, a dull hiss of moving air can be heard coming from behind the door.

"What's that sound?" I ask no one in particular.

Dr Roseline shrugs as she leans forward on her knees, out of breath from the short run. She isn't the only one - the air here is really, really painfully thin. "Maybe...something is...leaking?"

"It's being pressurised," soldier boy chips in suddenly. He turns and looks at our expressions. "What? You both may be super smart scientists, but I've spent more time in space than either one of you. I'm telling you, the room behind this is being pressurised."

So, the room was kept in vacuum? Why? At that moment, the hissing finally stops, and the door slides open by itself! So there's power left in this wreckage? Of course there is, the drone is still powered, isn't it? Speaking of which, the drone floats into the room, and just as I'm about to follow it, Wes grabs my shoulder and pulls me back. He gives me an angry glare before moving carefully into the room, holding tightly onto his gun. From the corridor, I can see the soldier carefully inspecting every corner of the room with his torch.

Finally, he gestures for us to enter. "It's safe."

We don't waste any time. The first thing I see once inside the room are cabinets. One is filled with chemicals in labeled glass jars. The other is filled with an assortment of biological samples. There's a computer terminal with a mini platform next to it attached to a ventilation shaft running up into the ceiling. Two lab coats hung on the wall next to the entrance. Unlike any of the other rooms in this ship, this room is perfectly preserved.

"It's a Research Laboratory," mutters Dr Roseline breathlessly. "The vacuum...it's to preserve the integrity of this room."

I nod in agreement. "But, why this room?"

It's then I catch a light blipping from the corner of my eye. It's from a huge box-shaped machine that occupies a large portion of the room. Curious, I walk towards it. It's the only thing on this ship that's turned on. There are some words inscribed on the side of the machine. I walk closer so I can read it.

CRYOGENICS.

I feel a chill down my spine. A large green button suddenly lights up on the panel in front of me. I have to know what it does. I need to know. Slowly, I reach up and push the glowing button. A hiss of air suddenly escapes noisily from the machine while a coffin-like tray slides out from the front!

My heart thumps painfully in my chest. Slowly, I lean forward and peek into the coffin.

"Meow."

A large brown cat leaps out of the coffin!

"Shit!" Wes curses and aims his torch at the cat. The cat stares at the soldier for a moment before bounding out of the room. Wes then shines the torch at me accusatively. "Liam, what did you do?"

Dr Roseline walks up to the machine and examines it curiously. "Oh my...they have a working crogenics technology! This is the most remarkable discovery! Earth will be most happy to reverse engineer this machine!"

"Dr Roseline, Liam just decrogenised a cat!"

I look at my feet guiltily. I can't really say anything in my defense. I did push the green button, after all. It was stupendously hypnotic, in my defense.

"Oh Lieutenant, dear, it's just a cat. Look, according to the display, there's one more we can decrogenise."

"Don't touch it!" the soldier snaps. "Look, no more decryonising anything, okay? Not until we know what's inside!"

"Oh, don't be silly, dear. It's just harmless science. Liam, you agree, don't you?"

"No he doesn't! And besides, this isn't a democracy!"

"That's right, Lieutenant. This is a scientific expedition, so I'm in charge," Dr Roseline asserts and turns to me. "But I run a democracy. You're the tie breaker, Liam. What should we do?"

I close my eyes. I want to find the cat. I want to keep exploring the ship. I want find out what's inside the machine. I want to go back to ground base and analyse everything we've gathered so far. There is no shortage of things I want to do. But, I should focus on one thing at a time. What's next?
