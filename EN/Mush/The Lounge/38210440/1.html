Welcome to a mini-StoryGame. Rules of an SG:

1. Author (in this case, me) writes a mini chapter of a few paragraphs, ending it at a decision point (DP).

2. Readers suggest what the protagist should do at that decision point, and roll a 1d100. They are allowed to support an existing suggestion by simply saying "X's suggestion, 1d100."

3. The highest 1d100 is then written into the next chapter by the author, and repeat from (1).

It's been a while... a couple days at least? I suppose it's only fair to catch you up as to what's happened in the past, just in case you need a little refresher. Once humanity came back from the Daedalus, everything returned to being “fine and dandy”... well, as long as you agreed with the regime. Due to a variance of the DNA strands in different Mush mutations, the administered cure didn't work for ALL Mush. Thanks to this, the current administration issued a “kill-on-sight” order for all Mush.

Since a single Mush misstep means death, I'm a loner by necessity. I've traveled the way I always have, from place to place, body to body, swapping whenever one gets too old or too weak. Originally, we needed a long time to recharge after transfers before we could summon the strength to do it again. However, through various mutations and adaptations, that barrier has been smashed. Most of what humanity thinks it knows about the Mush is wrong, and we're glad to keep it that way for as long as possible.

During one of my recent wanderings, I decided to watch an execution. As you might expect, I wasn't pleased at what I saw, and... well, let's just say that what I did there made news headlines worldwide. Not only that, but it shattered my original attitude of keeping my distance from others. So here I am, lying in wait near a warehouse. I wish I could pretend I regret it, but I really don't. It's nice, sensing others in your mind after it's been so long. After all those decades on my own, that feeling of something missing is finally gone.

As I looked around at the newborns trickling into our meeting place, I pondered how much we've adapted since the old days. Let's face it, we were better than the humans in many ways even before our persecution, but now... I've heard of Mush who could swap bodies in the blink of an eye, without needing to initially infect the target, or those who could freeze someone in place with their gaze. Most recently, I'd seen a Mush who could summon giant mushrooms, filled with infecting spores. I wasn't yet sure how she'd done it. I was planning to ask her later, but there were more pressing matters at hand.

I relaxed and thought about the warehouse district. This was one of the few warehouses that remained in the area, and because it was totally automated, few people came around here anymore. So long as no one was stupid enough to trip the security system, we'd have plenty of time to discuss the situation. I counted the newborns; there were six of them. Being totally new to their situation, they were suspicious, animalistic, closer to their baser natures. Yet, their eyes shone with a certain degree of inherent intelligence. That explained why these had made it through the aftermath of the failed execution. Red glanced over them, and her gaze softened.

“I suppose as their mother, it is my responsibility to name them.”

She walked towards the newborns. As she touched each on their forehead, she spoke in an ancient language I'd not heard in a long time—the Mush language. English had no real translation for the names they were given, but they were beautiful. I'll have to spend some time thinking of adequate translations later. As the newborns were named, they each nodded in acceptance. When she'd finished naming the last, she turned to me, and I could still see the fire in her eyes.

“So? What's your name?”

I tore my gaze away from her eyes to answer.

“Hei Lantern.”

She scoffed, rolling her eyes as she did so.

“Not the name of your current body, your name. What name did your mother or father give to you?”

I paused. It had been a long while... did I remember it still? “Iquas.” She noticed I'd translated it from the Mush language, but she didn't press further. If she was as old as she seemed, then she'd understand why.

“Just call me Red for now,” she teased. Not returning the favor was a bit rude, but I'd let it slide. After all, I hadn't really given her my name either.

“Now then, I'm going to ask you to follow me. You seem like a loner, you even smell human, so you probably haven't been around others for a while.” She turned and began to walk away. The other two Mush I freed looked at me and shrugged.

“Wait, where are you going?” I asked.

She stopped and looked back at me. “Where do you think? Do you really think we're going to sit back and let our kind continue to be slaughtered? To the resistance.” Her lips curled into a smirk before she continuing walking.

The... what?

In hindsight, it shouldn't have surprised me. The idea that many Mush would band together in order to oppose the bloodthirsty administration wasn't particularly outlandish. I guess after years and years on my own, I just didn't expect other Mush to be this organized.

Where Red lead us could've easily been easily overlooked on an ordinary day. It wasn't particularly...

I froze at the sound of the soft click of hammers in drawn guns. A figure stepped out of the shadows and greeted us in English: “Red, who're these people?” I glanced at the man. While his body was young, his mind was old, probably about as old as mine. Well, maybe a tad younger.

“They're with me. Six newborns, and... Iquas. He's the one in all the news reports.”

The man's eyebrows raised in interest, before he started laughing amiably. “Iquas, eh? Quite the stunt you pulled! We were about to move in ourselves, and then you just came blazing in out of nowhere! You gave us quite the start.”

I blushed, but then realized what else he'd said. Even if I had held back, they likely would have been fine. I started cursing to myself at blowing my cover for nothing, but then his gaze changed almost imperceptibly. Something in it now made me uneasy. He smiled and said, “You're welcome to stay with us for a while, Iquas. I can imagine it's been a long time since you've been around fellow Mush. You stink.”

Even though I knew the 'stink' bit was a playful jibe at how I'd been around humans so long, it still made me nervous. I couldn't shake the feeling that the guns were still pointed at my head, and the 'welcome' I'd received was hiding the fact I was being carefully corralled into a cage.

Suddenly, the strangeness dissipated, and the man's smile no longer seemed forced. “I'm Orion. Many call me Ori for short. Well, what do you want to do first, Iquas? Do you have any questions, or should I just give you a tour of the base and your new quarters? Something in particular you'd like to see, or are you feeling hungry? Just ask, and we'll do what we can. After all, you are the savior of the day, and we owe you!”
