I am not actually sure from what standpoint you are trying to argue "against the rules" here.

If it is an angle of practicality (i.e. whether the rules have reasons behind them, whether they are just and whether they are actually enforced - which all is definition of "applying the rules", as far as I can say), as seems to be the case, then stand assured the rules do apply. Every single one of them was created either for the game to be played as intended by its creator or to make sure the game is as fair as possible towards the players. And they are also effectively enforced - first by community who are, as individuals, free to report any activity suspected to be against them, and then by judges and moderators who evaluate these reports, have access to logs and make sure the offenders are sanctioned, if they actually broke any rules. It might not be the most objective and efficient system imaginable, but it surely is most objective and efficient system possible for browser game where development was concluded and all this work is done by volunteers.

If you are arguing from philosophical standpoint, then I'm afraid Mush is not the only game you might have problem. In chess, for example, what does force you to move with bishop only diagonally? Or in soccer - why can't you pick up a ball and run towards opponents goal, while others are trying to just kick it around? Who does stop you to break those rules?
The answer is social contract. If you agree to play a game, you, by default, agree to follow the rules. If you don't, then you don't play the game. You just ruin it for others. Sure, you can do homerules with your group of friends (and that's why we have castings on Mush) but when playing with random strange people, there's some default rule setting you are expected to follow. That's just the way games work.

And of course, there's a last reason you might have started this thread - to look for ways to bend the rules, find loopholes and rationale for breaking them. You wouldn't be the first and probably not even the last. And as with others before you, you will end up with deleted posts, locked thread and maybe even forum ban. So tread carefully.

There are true win conditions, actually. This is a survival and mush-hunting game, so the basic conditions are quite obvious. If you don't survive at a survival game, you lose. If you don't find the mush, you also lose, just like you lose if you play Cluedo and don't find the murderer. Same thing for the mush, they win if they survive and lose if they die.

Defining other conditions would be much too subjective, and every time people have tried to define win conditions, they failed to reach a consensus, because there are too many possible criteria.

As for rules, in some cases we have to have clear-cut rules (for instance, we cannot allow suicide at all, because if we said "suicide is allowed if the ship is really doomed", people would kill themselves left and right since "doomed" is a very personal notion). In other cases, we cannot define absolute rules. We can't forbid humans to kill other humans, it's impossible in an investigation games where innocents can be suspected and killed. But we can't completely allow it either, or griefers would have a field day.
