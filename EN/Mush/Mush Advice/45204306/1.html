Brief statistics class lecture:

We've all played around with dice and coins, flipping and rolling them around.

If I were to ask you what the odds are, if you flipped a coin that it would come up heads, why, you'd tell me 50%, most of you would.

But what would you tell me if I asked you what the odds were, that the second time you flipped that coin, that you would get heads?

Well huh! I didn't ask you about the first roll, so you can ignore that roll. So I'm only asking about a single roll, that happens to be the second roll... why that's also 50% chance to come up heads.

Ok, so what is the right answer, if I asked you to flip that coin ten times, and for each of the results to be heads? That's a bit more math... But how it works is (chance to get the desired result of first flip) x (chance to get the desired result of second flip) x (each flip handled like this until all are done), or 50% (0.5) x 50% (0.5) for ten times.

That comes out to 0.097%. in other words, you'd have to have 1000 people flipping a coin ten times to expect to find one who got heads all ten flips.

Except, probablility doesn't work like that. You might not have any in that 1000 (and a big chance of that) or you might even have 2... or 3 (tiny and SUPER tiny chance respectively). If you have more than 4 who got all heads... I'd be asking about unbalanced coins or people cheating somehow. Not because it's impossible - but because it's so very, very unlikely that it would happen.

Important stuff time: Some of those people got to 9 heads in a row, and had just one more flip to get to 10 heads in a row. What are the odds, after you get 9 heads in a row, that you'll get heads again on the 10th flip?


... How many of you said something like "... uhh, 1 in 1000, like you said"? Nope. Because they already got 9 of those ten. Those 9 are no longer random because they already happened, so you ignore those (unless you want to check the coin for fairness). We're only talking about this last flip.... a 50% chance to get heads instead of tails. Half of all the people who get 9 heads in a row will go on to get that 10th head too.



Ok, so that's the basic statistics. If you never play Chun, you have a 2/15 chance (supposedly) of being picked for mush. I say supposedly because those who know the game best have said that there's a weight system for it - and newbies have a lower chance to be selected as mush than experienced players. I don't know if that is true, and I don't know how the weight is applied, but I've seen plenty of inexperienced mush (granted, if you played 50 ships before becoming mush the first time.... you may still look like an inexperienced mush!).

So I'm going to assume Player X never plays Chun so has a 2/15 chance, or 13.33% chance to be mush on any ship.

To find out the odds of Player X playing 10 ships and never being mush on them we simply have to see what the odds of them being human each time are, then multiplying them together, ship after ship. So, 86.67% to the 10th power is: 23.92%.

Player X has about a 24% chance of playing non-Chun for 10 ships and never being alpha. This works even better in a big group: Get 1000 of us together, and about 239 of us have played non-Chun ten times in a row and not been alpha mush once.

How about 20 ships in a row, never being alpha? Ok, that's down to: 5.7%.

So after 10 more games, about 1/4th of the people who had not yet been alpha still had never been alpha, there is about 57 people cursing the RNG and thinking the game is totally unfair... meanwhile about 182 of the others suddenly find themselves unable to be alpha mush, and wonder about bad luck being catching.


Nope, it's all about probability and statistics. Because every ship you play, every time, you have about an 87% chance to not be alpha, even if you have already not been alpha 50 times in a row.

You haven't understood the calculations NERON made. Binomial distribution was used:

[n!/(k!(n-k)!)]*[(p^k*(1-p)^(n-k))]

The first part in [] is the number of possible paths and the second the probability for that path. With n trials, k successes, n-k failures and probability p.
For n=77, k=11 and p=1/7.5 you'll get those ~11,32%.
