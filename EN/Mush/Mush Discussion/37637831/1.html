The balance point, for the non-griefing players, is to treat it as if it were 'real'. We play Mush, we take the role of someone dedicated to winning despite the nearly impossible odds facing our characters; we thrive despite adversity and we never give up. That's a pretty useful core value; that's one of the centralmost concepts of the game, and it will hold each of us in great stead to cultivate within us. When the times get tough... well they have in the game described here. Monstrously tough, more unfair than ever - and this ship will never get any outside help, ever.

Give them total war to the extent the programming permits. Do not surrender in any way, but don't flinch from tactics that might kill you too.

One day you may be homeless and jobless. One day you or a loved one might have cancer. One day you may be on a plane that's being hijacked. One day you might be the only person in a position to be strong and try to save the situation, or you can just fold and collapse and let whatever happens happen. This game is about being strong, and finding a way until it is -impossible- for you to keep trying to find a way.

I've dealt with more than my share of murderships ruined by griefers, and like many of you, I also pay for Gold. Is it frustrating when a game is ruined through no fault of my own? Hell yes, it sucks, and I've never once suggested otherwise. It's my opinion that if the game were on a pay to play model, we'd probably have fewer griefers, since they usually wouldn't want to risk losing a paid account; but we'd probably have fewer players, overall. I don't know if that would even be financially viable for MT, but that's a topic for another discussion thread.

My default position in griefed ships has always been to keep pressing on as long as we can, finish Pilgred if possible, and then get back to Sol, if at all possible. Failing that, survive against the odds, and do whatever is necessary to keep advancing until both of the above conditions have been met. What do you do if you've been griefed that badly? Whatever you can to survive and get back to Sol, provided you don't commit Pathetic Suicide, don't surrender, and don't break any other rules in the process. That leaves a lot of leeway in what you can do. But if you bail while others want to keep fighting, you're letting the griefers win by default.

I acknowledge that griefers will always be present to ruin games, and we all know they don't care who gets affected in the process. At the same time, ALL players have to be held to the same standard by the SDF, and two wrongs don't make a right. This is the crux of the SDF position.

People who have had grenades or the microwave used as part of griefing attempts may want to hide these items before Mush selection, but it's sanctionable to do this. While it might be an understandable course of action...it's also something that we've sanctioned those people for doing. Their intent may have been to prevent griefing, but we still have to sanction them the same as a griefer who hides the Hacker Kit, Apron, or the other weapons before Mush selection.

The SDF does everything it can within its power to sanction griefers, and to keep them off your future ships. It's also an uphill battle for us at times, because when non-griefing players have their ships ruined, and they end up breaking the rules as a result, the SDF is still obligated to do the jobs we were assigned. It again comes down to two wrongs not making a right. Pathetic Suicide is sanctionable. Dying while in the course of advancing your team's goals is not. So, if you die because you spent your last AP killing the last Arack, and your sacrifice allows the ship to jump and keep going, it's not a Pathetic Suicide. (Granted, that's a rather extreme example, but a plausible one.)

In contrast, dying just because you want to get off the ship is what generally ends up being considered a Pathetic Suicide. So, if someone goes out in a patrol ship, doesn't fire at the Hunters, stays out there for a day, and is shown to be human after they die? They may not have intended to grief the ship, but it's still a Pathetic Suicide. Picking up and dropping the cat repeatedly is the easiest way to earn a sanction for Pathetic Suicide, and there is no wiggle room to justify doing that under the rules. Surrender and suicide were forbidden long before the SDF was created, and we can't make exceptions to this, not for ourselves, and not for anyone else.

If we excuse people who commit suicide because of griefing, then we're also going to have to make exceptions for people who kill themselves because they think there's "no chance to get to Eden", and commit suicide because of that. We'd have to make exceptions for people who committed suicide after all their rations were cooked, even if the rest of the crew managed to rally together and save them all. We'd have to make exceptions for every crew that had Mush steal the Pasiphae and/or the Icarus, and they subsequently want to give up. Ultimately, it then becomes a slippery slope in which players will want us to keep moving the goalposts.

"Why make an exception in this case, and not others?"

At that point, we'll be spending more time redefining what the rules are, rather than spending our efforts enforcing them. I hate griefing with a passion, and many of you already know just how hardline I am about griefing cases. At the same time, all players still have to be held to the same standard. Having your ship griefed doesn't make your own Pathetic Suicide suddenly acceptable...because if others want to press on, despite the odds, than you would be further ruining their chances of accomplishing that. The same goes for any rules being broken to "preemptively deal with griefing", such as hiding the Hacker Kit or weapons before Mush selection. We can't allow that, either.

Do I really have to explain why surrender is forbidden, despite the fact that it's in the rules? Alright, I will. If the Mush surrender, they deny the human team the challenge of the Mush hunt. If humans surrender, they hand the Mush an easy victory. As a PvP game, both teams deserve a worthy opponent; not one that will give up the moment things seem grim. Suicide also falls under the no surrender rule, as do Harmful Returns to Sol by human players.

For both teams, playing against one's team is explicitly forbidden. Griefing actions fall under this rule, obviously, as does human players killing other humans without any evidence. That wastes AP, causes a morale decrease, and when an active player is killed, everything they could have contributed to the ship is lost. Mush can't reveal who the other Mush are, nor may they ask to be cured. They also can't ask to be killed, "just because they don't like playing Mush." (Of course, that's explicitly treated as a surrender.)

So why do these rules exist? Ultimately, it comes down to this: Games must be played to their natural conclusion. If one team gives up or plays against their team, they deprive the other team of earning a victory, just as surely as a chess match in which one player removes every piece on their side except for their king. The victory for their opponent is all but guaranteed, but it sure as hell isn't earned. Griefing can also deprive both teams of an earned victory; as I've said on more than one occasion, "If griefers deal more damage to the humans than the Mush team, it doesn't FEEL like a win for the Mush team."
