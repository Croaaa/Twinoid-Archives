There's already plenty of randomness, mostly provided by the dynamics between crews. There are some things that you need to be able to plan for, such as when the rebel signals are coming in, and whether or not you'll be able to safely move the ship without losing that signal...that sort of thing. Or knowing what days certain Hunter types arrive, and when you'll need to tell your pilots to switch away from "Killing every hunter and collecting scrap" mode to "Kill only Aracks and Trax, and keep moving the ship, without collecting any scrap" mode.

One of the human goals is to survive as long as possible, and it's one of the most important ones. Being able to plan for some of the events to come increases that chance of survival. (And by the way, the game becomes more difficult based on the amount of AP spent, so some crews get very particular about not wasting AP. Because if you're playing well, you will never have enough AP for everything. Planning in advance greatly factors into avoiding this wastage.)

I find many of the surprises tend to come from the other players. When the Mush manage to convert someone, but none of the take Anonymush, the tension grows a bit more. I was horrified the time our Mush Frieda lied about the zones on the planet we were going to expo, reporting the Volcanoes zone as an Oceanic zone. We were already on the expo when I saw it, and called her out for it. Our team made it back in one piece, and she died shortly thereafter. But know that Volcanoes zones have a chance to instantly vaporize the whole expo team. From then on, I've always had someone double check the Astroterminal before the expo team leaves.

Even on "safe" expos, they can seem to be textbook...and then BAM! That Shipwreck zone you wanted to check out has a 32 strength alien in it, and you're all lucky to be limping away from the encounter without any casualties. I assure you, there is more randomness than you may be aware of, and having an idea of things that you can predict (not control, but predict) really helps. It's highly important when 19 Hunters show up, and you need to figure out if your pilots will have enough AP to kill just the ones you need in order to move this cycle.

What I'm getting at here is, get some more games under your belt, in particular ones that last beyond Days 10-12. I've no reason to lie about how tough it gets, and I think you'll see what I mean once you've made it that far, more than once.

I'm also not suggesting you're "wrong", either. Ships that make it past days 12-14 are fairly rare, because of the randomness element. You don't know what BPs you'll have on your ship, or what Mage books your Comms Officer will get from Xyloph. You don't know what skills your crewmates will bring to the ship, and you certainly won't know which of them is Mush. You might be surprised just how widely varied each voyage will be as a result.

If you want, take a look at my voyage history. I was on the first ship to make it to Day 15 after the launch (it displays as 14, in terms of full days completed.) I've also been on ships with griefers, that didn't last a week because of the havoc and damaged they caused. My worst ship was the last one in which I played Paola. Our Eleesha was responsible for the deaths of three people by the end of the first day, and we hadn't even managed to established comms yet. By the end of the second day, most of the crew was dead due to morale loss or injuries. On the third day, two humans and two Mush remained. I killed Ian with a critical hit from a blaster, which ironically also caused my morale to drop to zero, and I was dead via suicide at the end of that cycle. It was my worst ship, and yet there's no way that outcome could have been predicted at the start of the voyage.

I was also on this ship where we had literally no rations on Day 2. A bug wiped them all out, and we struggled to recover. This is also the same ship where our Mush Frieda sent us to a Volcanoes planet.

The variety is wild, my friend. No two voyages will ever play out the same, no matter how much you plan.
