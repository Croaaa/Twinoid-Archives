I don't see why upgrading drones, repairing or dismantling would be more difficult than piloting. Or maybe you consider that the difficulty of a character depends on the amount of AP they need ? But managing your AP is not the most difficult thing. Some jobs don't require to be very active, to know the game well and to think about your actions. You don't need to be an expert to repair something or to upgrade a drone. You do need to think when you're piloting, because you need to know when to kill hunters and when not to do it, how to do it, or which types of hunters to kill, depending on the situation (plasma shield, magnetic net, moves of the ship...). And you also have to be there for expos, know how to prepare for them and when to launch them (or sometimes re-launch).


While Jin Su for beginning moves only the ship (which direction he must get always) and gives morale / relay stuff. "Move only the ship" ? That's not an easy thing to do. Of course in practice it only requires a few clicks, but you can't do that if you don't have a clue what you're doing. You need to know the game if you want to move the ship on time, decide to escape hunters or to let pilots kill them, avoid killing someone who's still outside, allow the comms manager to decode rebel bases or decide to move and lose the base if needed, check for aracks, know if fuel has been loaded, know what planet you're going to, know when to leave orbit...

Giving morale isn't something you can't do without thinking either. Well, it can be, but then you have a commander who wanders the ship and gives random speeches everywhere, including in dorms where people have full morale thanks to the shrink, or just after the D5 rebel base. You need to know who needs morale and who doesn't, or when the situation requires speeches (when there's been lots of deaths, when there is no active shrink, when rebel bases have been lost, etc.).

And it is the same for missions. Again, you have to think, and to know what's happening around you, because the recipient of the mission depends on the situation (mush alive or not, emergencies such as hunters, astro, repairs or healings...) and you also need to know the crew well. Not like some people who only realize after three days that Chun is inactive or that the pilots are not Roland and Hua but Terrence and Frieda.

That's a lot to take into account for a beginner, not to mention the level of activity required.

To be fair this thread really only described skill floors, but not skill ceilings. You can be a "passable" character by hitting the floor, but a lot of characters with a low floor have a much higher ceiling to grow into.

A basic Gioele with Solid and maybe Paranoid is pretty easy to be "passable" at, but you could also be a Caffeine Junkie with Astro and Apprentice who is also the Commander because Jin died..... and then you've got the chance to be a real great guy if you're capable of it. The highest floor with a high ceiling is probably Terrence, just because even a Gold Terrence literally does not have enough slots for all the good skills he gets.

Though there's also a difference between mechanical difficulty and social difficulty. I think the floor for actually being a passable Chun is fairly high, and some of the dislike people have for playing Eleesha (at least with investigative skillset) comes from getting stuck with a bad Chun.

The argument of floors and ceilings is a good one - prime example of this is Kuan Ti. Your average Kuan just takes Designer and is often forgotten, mainly because his role with that is so minimal. So you have a great deal of freedom to pretty much do what you want. However, KT with Astro, Leader, Technician and Politician has to juggle so many roles. Braindead captain? You're in charge now. Mush paola? Get scanning. Janice/chun killed? Start revving up them speeches. Raluca drooling in her Turret? Grab a wrench, those O2 tanks aint gonna fix themselves.

I believe there were arguments ages ago about generalists vs specialists in Mush. I've always held the opinion that generalists are more AP intensive and tend to be a bit "harder" because your attention is likely to be pulled in so many different directions and I find a lot of merit behind that.
