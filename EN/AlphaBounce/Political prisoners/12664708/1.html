the way that most (if not all) good "ball breaker" games work, is that the pad is not represented in the coding by a rectangle, but rather by a whole bunch of tiny circles that overlap each other, and provide a collision system that is unique to where the ball hits the "pad". This allows for the multiple angles that you can achieve via hitting the ball with a certain part of your pad.

The way the circles are arranged is actually in an array more than likely, with the center being of course 0 (but with as small of a chance of hitting a specific collision circle are, you'll probably barely ever get this exactly) which would send the ball straight up with absolutely no angle whatsoever (if you were to have hit the ball with the center, and not moved your pad, it'd keep going up and down never deviating), while the ends of the array are set to be at the most extreme angle that the pad will ever achieve no matter how large or small. The rest of the collision circles are spread out with a formula that fills in the values for the angles the ball will shoot off from the pad to fill in the spaces equally from both ends to the center.

In this regard, it is thereby easier to control your ball's trajectory with a larger pad, because you can "tweek" your aim just a tad, and get much smoother results.

I don't know the exact angles or anything, but for the sake of simplicity here's an example:

if the left and right edges shot the ball off at 180 degrees (straight left or straight right, I know this isn't the case, but for the simple part of math it will make the example much easier) and the pad is so small that it only needs 3 circles to cover the entire thing, you'd only get either straight up (center) straight right or straight left ... if that pad were extended, and then needs 5 circles to cover it, then the far right/left would still shoot it off in the same angle, while the two new circles would be shooting the ball off at 45 degree angles. again, if the pad were extended and had to have 9 circles to cover it, the new circles would give you even more angles to shoot the ball off as...

Again, I KNOW that the angle for the extremes of the pad are NOT 180 degrees, but that was just for the ease of breaking it in half and stuff. So basically, in simple english: The longer the pad, the more collision circles you get to play with, and the angles get easier to "tweek" to your liking.

Sorry if I got anyone confused ^^
