Try emptying the flash cache. It's separate from the browser cache so clearing your browser cache/history doesn't affect it. A MMO flash game I used to play would get corrupt resource files in the flash cache every so often which would cause a huge slowdown.

The problem was presumably that the game would spend an inordinate amount of processing time trying to figure out what was in the corrupt resource. The result was usually invisible to the player (no obvious glitches and such) but the slowdown it caused was quite obvious. Clearing the flash cache would fix the problem upon a browser reboot.

Luckily clearing it is fairly simple; just right-click on alphabounce, click "settings," click "storage," move the slider to zero, click "ok," then move the slider back to whatever it was at. Restart the browser and check if the problem is still present or not.

Remember that there's a separate cache for Firefox and Chrome so clearing one won't affect the other. Also this only clears the cache for alphabounce - to clear other websites' data you'd need to either use the "global options" or go to the website of your choice and manually clear the data. Hopefully doing this will help at least some of you with the framerate problem.

Edit: People with unstable internet connections are much more likely to get corrupt files. That's why some computers have this problem regularly and others never experience it. Firewalls can also cause a similar effect if improperly configured.

Seriously guys? Every time someone has a problem you mention the most obtuse "solutions" possible? "Hey, my car seat has a tear, guess I need a new car."

Well considering clearing the cache made your FPS better that would imply you're getting corrupted data from somewhere. It's entirely likely that after clearing the cache, the game just redownloaded another corrupt file. If it's not your internet connection causing this problem (which isn't easily fixable) then the most likely culprits would be a poorly configured firewall or router.

Luckily the first is very easy to check; simply disable your firewall and clear your cache again. If you think you don't have a firewall, just know that Windows comes with one by default; type "disable windows firewall" into Google to learn how to disable it. If you do know which firewall you have, type "disable <firewall name> firewall" into Google instead. Once you've disabled it, clear the cache and restart the browser, then try to play another Alphabounce game. If this works then it's likely that your firewall is being too agressive and blocking non-harmful data. Remember to re-enable it after you're done checking.

If this doesn't work then things get a little harder. On routers there's also a firewall, known as a "hardware firewall." Just like the firewall on your compuer, it can block good data instead of only bad things. If you need help doing this then figure out what brand of router you have first. Then google "<router name> firewall settings" and temporarily disable it. Run another clear and try to play another game. If this works then the router firewall probably needs to be reconfigured. Again, remember to re-enable it after you're done.

If neither of these things works (or the problem comes back later after you've changed the firewalls) then the issue is probably too difficult to fix. There are a number of things that could still be causing it but none of which I can easily explain how to solve. But hopefully the previous two solutions will fix your problem, and if they do then congratulations, you just troubleshooted a computer problem.

If you're going to follow the advice to disable your firewall, just be aware that your computer is under nigh constant attack. It can take just a few seconds for an unprotected computer to be compromised. Anytime you disable your firewall you should make sure that you are disconnected from the internet.

If a person isn't awayre that they have the default Windows firewall or a router firewall, then it isn't "a poorly configured firewall" which is the issue. The default Windows firewall for XP and later is fairly tame. The same applies for the firewall on most vendors routers. If they haven;'t been tinkered with an altered, which won't have happened if they are unknown, then they aren't likely to be the source of a problem.

What would accomplish everything you've suggested without requiring turning off your firewall would be a simple reboot of both the computer and access point/router. Much easier and safer than turning off either or both firewalls protecting your computer.
