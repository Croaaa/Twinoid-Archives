I wondered what is wrong when I've built second bridge across the planet and perpendicular to my first bridge. They didn't cross in second point! "How can it be that two meridians do not cross on a sphere? Maybe it's not a sphere?" - I asked myself. By the way, it's not a simple task to create a good sphere model, and I just wanted to see how developers made it. But no, they didn't make sphere at all.

Luckily, I once pressed "F" key instead of "D" and saw some service information. The most interesing thing is the block with coordinates: x, y, z and angle. I ran along my first bridge - the y coordinate was on 62 (plus-minus 1 - the width of the block, and my bridge is 2 blocks wide). The second bridge: x = 97. Both x and y change from 0 to 255, and 256 jumps to 0. The map looks simple enough: it's a square 256x256. And the "real geometric body" is made by connecting edges, so 256 is 0 and we made a circle. Do you recognize the thing? It's a torus! You could know it as a "donut", but mathematicians call it "torus" nevertheless. And it's much easier to model this than sphere.

Honestly, it's somewhat "abstract" torus: it has no "inner" (by the hole) or "outer" sides, so no side is shrunk or stretched. You cannot build a tower and see mountains hanging to you from the top (as it could be on the "inner" side, if it were a real torus). You cannot dig many holes through the center and expect they'll cross in one point. You can try to dig one tunnel in the very center (z=0), but generally there can be many such tunnels, and they won't cross if they go along one of coordinates, they'll just be parallel.

Finally, I built 2 more bridges: with x=225 and with y=190. Felt myself like a mountain dwarf: dig, build, dig, build...
