Chapter 10: Enter Grumhel (special thanks to Killer60)

Very soon, Master Hydargol got exhausted and could barely fight any more. The korgons were getting tired, too. There was nothing I could do, but expect the worst.
But then, I saw the eyelid of Thrust slightly open. He lifted his head, looked around...and realized the danger. Quickly, he got to his feet, and started to perform a skill I had never seen him perform: Flash wave! (attacks 3 enemies with a lightning power of 10: this skill is available to the French and Spanish servers)
Thrust electrocuted the pirhanoz from range with aggression until the fish could not take it any longer and retreated.
Thrust was standing on his feet alive and strong again. I threw my arms around him in gratitude for being alive and the korgons did the same, relieved to have the wanwan back.
“It seems Thrust had learned Flash wave while in his dream. I’m impressed,” Master Hydargol said.
After we reached the Edge of the Forest, we parted with the Zenzen master. Amigo uttered, “Home, bitter home,” at our arrival. The forest was dark — little light could penetrate through the canopies.
I kneeled down to the river for a drink, but Amigo stopped me:
“Wait! This water is full of bacteria that only korgons can drink.”
“So...where can we get water from?” I asked.
“You get squeeze the water from this moss,” he showed me a patch of moss that grew at the bank of the river. The moss was filled with special chemicals that killed the bacteria from the water, so it was safe to drink from. That Thrust and I drank from while the korgons drank from the river.
Then, we continued on the mission. I knew that Nicolas Mulot was living somewhere in this area, so we searched. It was then I remember in my studies of Grumhel that is was the inhabitance of the Gate Guardians. The deeper we would move into the forest, the higher risk we would put ourselves in. So I urged everybody to keep ourselves as close to the edge as possible.
Finally, we found it — Nicolas Mulot’s house was located in a small opening in the forest. The sunlight could beat down freely in that area, so it was a relief to step out of the mess of trees for a moment.
I knocked on the door to Nicolas’ house...but no answer. I knocked again, but this time, I opened the door myself...only to look upon a terrible sight.
The study room, in which Nicolas was supposed to be in, was completely destroyed! Papers were scattered and torn apart and bookshelves were shredded. The only thing that seemed to be intact was a cookbook with a weird name. The other thing was more gruesome, though. It was a flag, a flag that had the following written on it: “The Conquerors”! I looked to the right and noticed a blanket covering something. When we went over to take a closer look, we could not bare the scene...a fallen gorilloz...
Evidently, Benroy had told the conquerors about Joe’s message. They came and harassed the place, capturing Nicolas and leaving his gorilloz for dead.
We had to leave; get back to Dinoland and figure out a way to defeat the conquerors. So we left the house, exited from the front door, only to find ourselves surrounded by a hundred masked korgon avengers all wearing the same conqueror’s flag on their backs like a cape. Up in front was their leader.
“Hey, isn’t that Krogin Hood?” Amigo wondered aloud.
The avenger leader stepped up and announced, “I am Krogin Hood! Leader of the Merry-Korgons! Surrender your possessions and we’ll spare your lives!”
“But I don’t have anything you could use!” I said, but Krogin didn’t listen.
“Silence, liar! Prepare to face the wrath of the conquerors with my Merry-Korgons! An enemy to the conquerors is an enemy to us!”
“Thrust! Flash wave!! Repetition!!”
Thrust fired chain-lighting at the avengers as they attacked. The rest of us took part in fending them off from a closer range. Nevertheless, there were simply too man avengers.
“Thrust can’t you take out more than three at a time?” I asked in desperation, but Thrust shook his head.
The avengers drove us back into the house. We were now completely surrounded with nowhere to go. For such small creatures, they posed a big threat when in groups.
But then, Thrust had an idea! When things seemed to go completely against us, Thrust snatched the cookbook in his mouth and displayed its title to the lot of the avengers. They stopped attacking! Now they were yelling and panicking and making their way out the house, into the deep forest! (Amigos stole a felled avenger’s baseball bat afterwards)
“No wonder,” I said, taking the book from Thrust’s mouth, “100 Ways to Cook a Korgon.”
Amigo snatched the book from my hands and uttered, “That’s sick! Top 100 of the best of the best man-chow cuisines. (Opens the book) Hey look, it’s uncle Steve!”
That night, it was raining hard. We had left the house of Nicolas to continue our journey. The korgons were used to the rain, but I understood how Thrust hated it. So I built a humble roof made of sticks and leaves for Thrust to sleep under (it was the best I could build). But I had no time to build one for myself, so I just lied under the rain, hoping that it would stop soon.
But soon, I felt a soft tug on my sleeve. When I looked, it was Thrust, soaking wet, tugging me to sleep inside the hut I had built for him. I couldn’t understand why Thrust refused to sleep under the protection of his worst enemy.
He stopped tugging me when he realized that I wouldn’t move because of tiredness. Instead of going inside the hut, he curled himself to sleep right beside me. I was touched — I guess love had proven itself to conquer anything. I would never forget this moment...

After that night, we traveled deeper still and then stopped to rest for another night. Amigo told that snakes inhabited the area, so I pitched up hammocks with vines so the snakes wouldn’t eat us.
“Aye,” Amigo said as we slept, “we’ve also got to watch out for the batwings...”
“BATWINGS!!!” Bob jumped all of a sudden and caused quite a commotion until Amigos bonked his head with the baseball bat.
“He’s quite skittish over batwings. I remember a korgon, named Junior, who was the first to be bitten by a batwings. After that, his mind went haywire and he could never be himself again. P.S., Junior is Bob’s second name.”

There are so many Gate Guardians here.”

“They’re the evilest creatures of Grumhel. Let’s just tiptoe our way out.”
At that, I took off my shoes and trod like a wanwan. With every loud step, a Guardian would shift on the ground before it went back to sleep again. It took is 30 minutes just to pass one kilometer and we were getting weary of tiptoeing. Then Amigo decided to whisper:
“Hey, man, if we see a bat...”
But he was shut up by Amigos, “In front of Bob, don’t say ‘batwings’.”
“BATWINGS!!!” Bob screamed and woke up the Guardians!
Their size was unmatchable! They were like angry gods of the forest who thought of all dinoz and humans enemies. One Guardian approached us from the front while three did from the back. I could see other Guardians from afar, as well...but now we had to act!
“Split up!” I shouted.
We took three different routes. Thrust accelerated past two Guardians, The three korgons just barely survived an earth-shattering punch and pressed on, and I ran into the thick woods to keep my chaser from speeding towards me.
I could see Thrust making a huge distance between himself and the Guardians, and then he accelerated back at them with focus, punching a hole right through both at once!...but it did not affect them! Then he charged right back on the open path and attacked my chaser, trying to electrocute it, but with no affect either.
As for the korgons, it seemed luck favored them most. Their little legs could not deliver them quickly, but they always escaped every Guardian’s blow.
My chaser was now paying attention to Thrust (who was running away at normal speed). So I turned back, made a lasso out of my rope, and hurled it over the Guardian’s head and passed the rope to Thrust as he accelerated back past the monster pulled onto the rope with such force that it took the Guardian to the ground! Then the punctured Guardians appeared, but Thrust leaped through their holes and tied them together. As expected, the two beasts, that could not comfortably attack the wanwan, grabbed the rope and swung it (without any idea what the weight at the end of the lasso was), missing Thrust and smashed the Guardian into pieces on a huge boulder.
The korgons finally met their dead-end — they were surrounded. As Thrust ran to save them, the Guardians smashed the ground where he ran, but he stealthily zigzagged pas their blows and caught the korgons.
From there, we ran as fast as we could, having confused the Guardians with Thrust’s acceleration skill. We ran until we met the edge of the Jumin River. The current was too strong for any human to beat, and so was the same with wanwan. But the korgons had no problem, as they demonstrated top us. That’s when an idea struck me.
“You guys paddle on, then,” I ordered.
“And leave you behind?” Amigo objected.
“I have a plan.”
And so the korgons paddled away as I withdrew the remaining piece of rope I had and handed it to Thrust, took out my “surfing board”, and ordered, “Thrust. Accelerate!” we sped away from the river towards the two Guardians that had been bound. I timed the moment of impact...waited...readied my free hand...and grabbed the end of the lasso. Like an overcharged playground swing, Thrust’s acceleration boosted us into the air...and now we were flying across the Jumin River as if launched from a catapult!...and slammed into the ground to safety...


Chapter 12: The Korgon Camp

When I finally opened my eyes, I looked upon another weird incident: first there were Gate Guardians, and now there were a thousand copies of Amigo, Amigos, and Bob! I was surrounded by a thousand korgons who meant no harm, unlike the conqueror avengers, but now I had a hard time looking for my companions.
“Amigo! Amigos! Bob!”
“We’re right here, ya muck!” Amigo said right beneath me — the korgons were tied to my leg!
“What happened?”
“Sorry, senor, but I’d like you to meet our brothers and sisters, all victims of the batwings’ bite...”
“BATWINGS!!!” Bob shouted, but Amigo shut him up with his words and then continued:
“They think that your leg is a wooden pole to which to tie us...we’re escapees from this camp, see.”
“Wow...so, who’s Junior?”
“They’re all Junior...”
With a broken leg and arm from the fall, I skipped through the crowd to look for Thrust. Surprisingly, he was receiving better hospitality than the korgons tied to my leg. They were shading him, giving him fresh water, and offering him meat. But at first sight of me, he dashed and jumped into my arms, welcoming me back to the solid world.
“Okay, everybody, coming through, coming through,” a young woman made her way towards us and gave an apologetic look, “Sorry for the korgons, poor things...”
She quickly untied our companions and gave them quite a scolding for leaving the camp. The korgons pretended to be sorry, but I could tell she was driving them crazy.
I didn’t consider Diana Korgsay such a bad person as our korgon friends had described her. She was quite hospitable towards us and all the korgons in the camp. However, she did hold a stubborn belief that our korgons, just like the rest in the camp, were victims of the batwings’ bite.
Miss Korgsey tended to our wounds...at least. Having a rest from the stampede of Guardians did not make up for the delay we were going to wait to get back to Dinoland. One night, at the campfire, I asked Amigo more about the Guardians.
“They were supposed to be protectors of the forest from intruders, as Article 7 of the Grumhel Constitution says,...”
(A constitution without a nation? O_o)
“...but they think it means they can rule the forest as they please and there is no way to stop them. At least they’re all located across the Jumin River. Or else they would always be after us to tear our limbs apart for dinner...and sometimes lunch — they’re vegetarian during breakfast time, see.”
“Okay...but what about Miss Korgsey, she isn’t such a bad person.”
“O, so thinks you, but I don’t like her much. I’m sick and tired of being treated like I’ve been bitten by a batwings! You don’t know how relieved I was when I won three tickets to Dinoland. It’s just the war wasn’t first-class treatment. Next time, I’m going to the Magnetic Steps!”
That was the end of the conversation.
A few months had passed, my bones were fixed...and it was winter. Not just any winter—it was the coldest winter I had ever experienced! Korgons huddled together to stay warm while I and Thrust stood close to the fire for all the time we could. I overheard the weather forecast on Korgsey’s radio:
“Hello, everybody,” said the weatherperson, “This year’s winter is extremely chilly. The average daily temperature has gone down to -60 degrees! No one knows whether this occurred naturally, or it is a man-made incident, because it’s impossibly cold. In fact, it’s so cold, that the body of the great Volcano has almost literally become an iceberg! Although the Dinoland government tells the people that this winter is survivable and that it will help slow down the conqueror’s progress, many say that this is the coldest winter for the last 1000 year! Who knows? This could be the dawn of the ice age that wiped out the first dinoz that ever walked the face of the earth...”
Quite agreeable, my feet were getting numb and so were my fingers. To warm up, Thrust and I decided to play tag (he kept getting away to fast). After that, I decided to teach him a new skill: hermetic aura. After many attempts and encouragements, Thrust finally pulled it of — it was like a whitish force field had formed around him as an indestructible barrier — and just in time, too! All of a sudden, the ground shook and we saw the Guardians just 100 meters away!
The Jumin River had frozen!
Quickly, I and our three korgons hid under Thrust’s hermetic aura and we clawed to the edge of the camp. As soon as we hit safety, I ordered Thrust to save as many korgons as he could and fight the wretched Guardians. But he could do little to rescue them, much less fending off the Guardians.
Amigo turned to me and pointed at the mountain, the foot of the great Volcano, “This is our chance! We have to run for it!”
I gave Amigo an angry look, answering him, “We are not leaving these korgons to their deaths! We have to stay and fight.”
“We have a mission, remember? If the conquerors succeed in Dinoland, they’ll succeed anywhere! We’re running out of time, man!”
He was right, although I was sure I’d be stoned for this. There was nothing we could do to help the korgons and we would die if we did, leaving Dinoland no hope of surviving the war. But I promised to myself that when we took care of the conquerors, we would come back.
I sighed and called out, “Thrust! Abort!!” and we made our way up Mt. Big All-Hot...

He generously gave me a piece of his coral stones, and then continued, “Is that it?”
“Well, yea, that’s all we really need.”
“Oh, come on, you’re too nice! Come, you and your buddies, we’re having a big dinner tonight! It’s already getting late, and you don’t want to be swimming in the ocean at dark, believe me.”
I refused several more times, but he finally persuaded me to join them. I guessed Thrust and the boys (and also the hippoclamp) had deserved a well-earned meal and rest for going through all the things for me (for most part of the journey, I felt I was giving orders all the time).
We sure did eat well. The Atlanteans were so generous people that I could almost not tell them apart from my kind. After we ate, they showed us our room where we could sleep for the night, and there we rested. The next day, I asked the same miner who invited me to stay with them about how to navigate through the islands to get to Gumhel Forest.
“It won’t be so easy,” he explained, “But you must keep going east from here until you reach the Mutant Falls. Beware, though, the swamp standing in between poses a threat to those who cross it. There are even rumors that tell of a creature that dwells in the quagmire, devouring the brave and foolish. I suggest you go around it; there are plenty of islands to take a pit stop.
“Once you reach the Mutant Falls, talk to Master Hydragol, the senior Zenzen monk. He will help you get to the forest. Head north from the Falls and you will reach the Edge of the Forest! Sorry I can’t tell you anything more. I’m limited to these islands.”
“That’s okay.”
I thanked him and gave gold nugget of the few that I had, and we proceeded eastward. We all felt refreshed and merry. The watery path was peaceful and smooth and we could easily make our way to the next island. The Coral stone proved itself useful to making a fire to cook our lunch (pirhanoz) and to dry the two letters I had.
We were ready to go forward again…but this time we were not alone…
As soon as we mounted on the hippoclamp, a strange stir caused ripples in the water. A moment later, something jetted up from the water and flipped us over! The creature roared and slashed its raptorial legs (the claws of a mantis) at us, but its eyesight was only good underwater.
“Trust! Focus!!” I roared as soon as we made it to solid ground.
He stood ready and his fur slowly collected glow. The first spark of lightning blurted our, but when it did, the lightning attack Thrust was generating backfired immediately at the drenched wanwan…and he fell unconscious!
“Thrust!!”
The eeloz slashed again…


Chapter 8: The Swamp

The eeloz slashed its raptorial leg again; we dodged it and fell back further out on land. But it followed us, using its claws to crawl, something I had not expected, at which point a snatched a random stick and started fighting the best I could.
The korgons dragged Thrust to a safe distance…and stared arguing again about how to revive him.
I, however, was struggling in the fight. As soon as the eeloz had learned my moves, it caught the stick when I attempted a strike and disarmed me. All that came to mind was to dodge the creature’s attacks. Then, I remembered in my dinoology studies that an eeloz’s weakest point is its lungs. Those I grabbed and tugged onto fiercely, drawing my knife, and about to stab it, but the eeloz electrified me before I even could. The shock left me paralyzed on the ground. I couldn’t move a limb, but could only see the sea monster close up on me and open its jaws.
The korgons looked at the situation and started to get desperate.
“O man, what to do?!” Amigo looked at random direction until his eyes stopped at the hippoclamp, “I have an idea!”
The three sped up to the hippoclamp and took the rope off of it. Unaware of what was going on, it stared at the three korgons climbing onto it back.
Amigo continued, “Okay, on my mark: one, two, THREE!!!”
All at once, they jumped and landed on the hippoclamp’s back, causing it to cough out a gust of mistral and blew the eeloz off of me.
“Yea! We’re deadly!!” cheered the korgons while jumping on our ferry (it must have been hard on the hippoclamp).
I recovered at that instant and ran towards the hippoclamp, commanding it to aim at the eeloz and shouted, “[b]Gel!![/b]”
The hippoclamp pelted a jet of liquid from its mouth that hit the eeloz with bull’s-eye accuracy. The liquid quickly hardened into a jelly and then hardened even further as the creature tried to free itself from it. A second later, and the jelly was rock-had and froze the monster inside of it.
I ran up to the unconscious Thrust, almost weeping, shouted, “Thrust! I’m so sorry!”
I picked him up in my arms and attempted to shake him awake, but he didn’t. My heart pumped as if it was ready to stop, but the korgons tried to comfort me:
“He’s not dead. Don’t worry, man. We can revive him.”
I tried my best to hide my tears. Amigo’s words did comfort me a bit…if not completely. But if I hadn’t made the mistake of telling Thrust to focus while he was soaking wet, none of this would’ve happened. It was no wonder a wanwan’s worst enemy was water.
We continued eastward, following the morning sun that was almost over our heads, indicating noon was approaching. Finally, we could see the Sticky Swamp in the horizon. Out of curiosity, I lead the hippoclamp to swim closer…until we arrived at its edge. Amigo was against it:
“Have you gone mad? Turn back while you still can!”
But I didn’t respond.
“Listen to the Atlantean! Remember what he said? Remember what they say: ‘Curiosity killed the cat’…wanwan…knocked unconscious…God, I’m stupid!”
“There are all sorts of plants in the swamp. One of those could help revive Thrust. We just need to hind it and get out.”
“You’re stupid!” shouted Amigo and supported by Amigos.
But in the end, we proceeded forward, with every muscle in our bodies on the ready. We searched and search…and searched some more. I gave my best efforts to finding the plant needed for Thrust, but everything was not right. The korgons searched too, but with less enthusiasm.
We had to spend a night in the marsh. There was nothing we could find edible there. To relieve some of my boredom and fear, I looked through the two papers. I looked through the wanted list, but the dim firelight did not give me brightness to observe it carefully. I looked though the parchment from Joe, which I tried reading with difficulty, but managed:

Dear Nicolas,
Long time no messages! You haven’t written to me so often every since you graduated from my courses.

So Nicolas Mulot was Joe’s former student.

You may or may not have heard of this, but the second war has already started. Do you remember the costly victory we obtained then? Well, we cannot afford to suffer a larger debt this time. It is my fear that Dinoland will fall in the hands of the conquerors.
However, if you meet a young lad, roughly in his 20s with a yellow, wild wanwan, then I sent him. I want you to take a sample of the gluon he will bring you and research its properties, for I believe that there is a substance in the gluon that makes it powerful and indestructible until it fades of age. I would love to do it myself, but I am far too old to do such work anymore.
But I fear though, unless this message has somehow found its way to you by miracle, it is very possible to be intercepted. If you do receive Skora, please keep her with you until the war is over. You know how precious she is to me.

And then from that sentence, the words became sloppy and hard to read, but this is how I interpreted it:

If she is intercepted, know eeloz azaldouip

Whatever that was… I felt sorry though for the ole timer. What would he feel if he found out?
The next morning, we searched again…hour after hour, but soon I realized: I wasn’t a botanist.
Amigo stepped forward to, seemingly freaked out of the mushy ground and foggy view of the quagmire, “What do you think you’re doing? Guessing is so not going to help, man.”
“You’re right,” I sighed, “I’m a dinoologist, not a botanist.”
“Oye, let’s just quietly made our way out by following our footprints.”
But the problem was the footprints we left had vanished! What was more, Thrust, the other korgons, and the hippoclamp had all vanished!
Amigo gave an irritated glance and uttered, “I hate it when we have to play hide-and...WHOA!!!”
“Amigo!!!”
A gust of mist swallowed Amigo into its body. Before I could break into a run to find him, the wind hurtled me of my feet, as well. I landed onto the soggy earth, covered in the stinky mud as mist twirled around me. I felt scared, to tell the truth, as I could tell from my legs twitching uncontrollably as I stood up.
Then the mist ceased twirling. I ran to and fro in search of my comrades, but without success. Every time I thought I was close to one of them, the mist turned aggressive and threw me off to a random direction.
I didn’t know if I could control any part of limbs. All my muscled seemed to go haywire as I struggled just to stand up. And as soon as I did, the winds howled and encircled me like a cyclone. The range of my vision became so limited that the mist only let me see the ground beneath.
“Guys! Guys!!” I shouted, but no one responded.
And then the worst I could possibly imagine happened: I started seeing small sparks of in the air. These sparks grew to become lightning bolts, interconnected by the slenderest molecules of air. Now the bolts encircled me until they created a ring of lightning that slowly closed on me.
And in front of me appeared…a pair of bright, transparent eyes…


Chapter 9: The Monks of the Lily Pad

The bloodcurdling eyes flashed at me at that moment...and the next, they vanished. Still, the lightning walls were getting closer. I was too stunted to think straight...what that creature could be. Adrenaline rushed through my veins and into my brain...and that adrenaline helped! It was a cloudoz! We were inside a cloudoz! That explained everything. I knew what to do:
“Hippoclamp!” I shouted in hopes that it could hear me, “Absolute zero!!”
Fortunately, it did hear me: the air grew colder and then icecaps formed on the puddles and streams of the swamp; the walls that surrounded me diminished to nothingness. Okay...

“That’s a big wound,” he observed the battle scar on Thrust’s chest, “We’re going to hold a healing ritual this evening for him, if you’d like that.”
