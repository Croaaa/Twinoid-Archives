This is a game based on this thread, with revised rules for exciting and balanced play for any number of players.

Each player is either a Comrade (friend) or a Saboteur (enemy), which is secretly assigned by a GM.


Objectives

The Comrades don't know who is a Saboteur. Their objective is to complete the Mission with a Team of players who are all Comrades within a certain number of Attempts.

The Saboteurs know who all the other Saboteurs are. They're allowed to discuss plans secretly. Their objective is to sabotage the Mission by getting at least one Saboteur on the Team for every Mission Attempt.


Before the game begins

Players must express an interest to play and/or GM, as well as any ideas for a theme (see below). To begin the game, you need a GM, 4-10 players, and a set of parameters for balanced game play (see below).

The GM must secretly assign each player's role and inform them privately. The GM should also start a PM with all the Saboteurs together. The GM may optionally write a narrative introduction for the chosen theme. Then the game begins with the first Attempt.


Election

Each Attempt starts with an election of a Leader. To vote, every player should post a list of all the players they support, not including themselves. To be an eligible candidate, a player must:

  • not self-nominate
  • vote
  • not be the last player to vote
  • have enough supporters (see below)

Any player can change their vote at any time, but a changed vote will not make the last voter eligible. There may be multiple players eligible, and any of them can become the Leader at any time by declaring an Attempt. The vote remains open until the Attempt is declared.


Attempt

To declare an Attempt, the Leader must choose a certain number of their own supporters that they will take with them as Team Members on the Mission. When an eligible candidate posts a valid Team, with a clear statement that they are making an Attempt, they become the Leader of that Attempt. Then the Mission is underway, and the Team cannot be changed.


Outcome

Then the GM must reveal the Mission outcome. If no Saboteurs were on the team, the Comrades win, and the game is over. Otherwise, the Attempt is a failure. The GM may optionally write a thematic narrative of the success or failure, but the narrative must not contain any hints about who is a Saboteur.

When an Attempt fails, the next Attempt begins immediately with a new election. Leadership and support do not carry over.

If the Comrades fail every Attempt they're allowed to make, the Saboteurs win.




(GM stuff below here. Players don't need to read.)


THEME

The GM may choose a theme for the game, and rename the player's roles accordingly, such as Humans and Aliens, Angels and Demons, Cops and Criminals, Programs and Viruses, etc. Other terms such as Attempt, Leader, Supporter, Team, Mission, etc. may also be replaced with theme-relevant terms. Players may roleplay within the theme.


PARAMETERS FOR BALANCED GAME PLAY

The fairness of this game depends on several factors:

  • the number of players
  • the number of Comrades
  • the number of team members on each Attempt (including the Leader)
  • the number of Attempts allowed

Incorrect numbers would give one team an unfair advantage. Here are the balanced parameters for 4-10 players:

4 Players:

  • 2 Comrades, 2 Team Members, 3 Attempts

5 Players:

  • 3 Comrades, 3 Team Members, 5 Attempts

6 Players:

  • 3 Comrades, 3 Team Members, 10 Attempts
  • 4 Comrades, 4 Team Members, 7.5 Attempts

7 Players:

  • 4 Comrades, 3 Team Members, 4.375 Attempts
  • 5 Comrades, 4 Team Members, 3.5 Attempts
  • 5 Comrades, 5 Team Members, 10.5 Attempts

8 Players:

  • 4 Comrades, 3 Team Members, 7 Attempts
  • 5 Comrades, 4 Team Members, 7 Attempts
  • 6 Comrades, 5 Team Members, 4.666 Attempts

9 Players:

  • 5 Comrades, 3 Team Members, 4.2 Attempts
  • 6 Comrades, 4 Team Members, 4.2 Attempts
  • 6 Comrades, 5 Team Members, 10.5 Attempts
  • 7 Comrades, 5 Team Members, 3 Attempts
  • 7 Comrades, 6 Team Members, 6 Attempts

10 Players:

  • 5 Comrades, 3 Team Members, 6 Attempts
  • 6 Comrades, 3 Team Members, 3 Attempts
  • 6 Comrades, 4 Team Members, 7 Attempts
  • 7 Comrades, 4 Team Members, 3 Attempts
  • 7 Comrades, 5 Team Members, 6 Attempts
  • 8 Comrades, 6 Team Members, 3.75 Attempts
  • 8 Comrades, 7 Team Members, 7.5 Attempts

The rows that are NOT italic (with whole-number Attempts) are perfectly balanced. The italic rows are almost balanced, but the Attempts must be rounded to a whole number. Rounding down gives a slight advantage to the Saboteurs. Rounding up gives a slight advantage to the Comrades.

The number of supporters must be at least one less than the number of Mission Team Members, and at most two-thirds the total number of players. When this parameter is low, the game tends to be a fast-paced power-grab. A higher number would require more deliberation and cooperation.

The number of players can't change after the game has started. If a player drops out or goes inactive, they must be replaced by a new player, assigned the same role.

I should probably explain why those rules of eligibility are necessary. The election is like a mini-game-within-a-game. Most players want to win the election. But in the original rules, if you vote for somebody else, you're probably not going to win the election. So there was actually an incentive to not vote. This became an observable Nash equilibrium. Many times, players would post comments during the election, but not vote. This caused the game to be slow and boring, which simply isn't fun. So I changed the rules to require every player to vote before they can be elected. But if a person can vote and then immediately become elected, they still have an incentive to wait for other players to vote for them. So the rule that the last voter isn't eligible is necessary to create an incentive for players to vote as soon as possible. This eliminates the stonewalling Nash equilibrium, and makes the game flow more quickly.

I also observed a lot of copycat voting for a player simply because they were already likely to win. But that's not a very good way to collectively make a good decision. It's also rather boring, knowing the outcome of the vote long before it's official. The multiple support rule ensures a more robust voting process, and creates an exciting sense of suspense and urgency, because there's sometimes a race between multiple eligible candidates, but there are cases when a candidate would want to wait for a certain supporter, so they have to decide which is more important. Risky dilemmas are fun!

So the new voting rules enable the group to reach a better decision more quickly, with some added excitement. That makes the game more fun than the stalling and predictability of the old rules.
