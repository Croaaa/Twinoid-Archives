ArcenMann, 8 is a pretty shitty question but honestly not even close to being the worst I've encountered on a standardized test. 10 actually isn't. It's a reading comprehension and logical deduction question, just like most every question about a passage. Hear me out here.

10. What would have happened if the animals had decided to cheer for the hare?
a. The pineapple would've won the race.
b. They would have been mad at the hare for winning.
c. The hare would have just sat there and not moved.
d. They would have been happy to have cheered for a winner.

a and c are both illogical in that they wouldn't have happened. These would make sense if the animals cheering for a competitor made either the other competitor lose, or their competitor stay still. These are actually both post hoc ergo propter hoc errors (basically chronology does not indicate causation, wikipedia explains it better). This question is great because it is teaching kids to avoid these errors. In order to understand answers b and d, an excerpt from the passage is crucial. "So the animals, wanting to back a winner, all cheered for the pineapple." Bam. We now know both that they would not be mad at the hare for winning (because they had cheered for him) and that they would have been happy because the hare won (again because they had cheered for him and he had won). This conclusion only requires having read the line I quoted and inferring that when one achieves one's wants, one becomes happy, rather than mad. Again, the key thing on this question was reading comprehension, though logical reasoning played a decent part.

Of course, on the actual test one would do that in 20-30 seconds basically off of intuition rather than by looking up logical fallacy names on Wikipedia. I think we can all agree that question 8 is a bad question, if not a terrible question.
