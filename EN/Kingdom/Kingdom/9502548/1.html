Erem
Possible sequence of events:
1) You see your master's general at your capital but no garrison, so decide to revolt;
2) Zen69x suspects you may be thinking of revolting and transfers some catapults from his general to your capital;
3) You click the button to start the revolt and, seeing an opposing army, wrongly assume that it's his general you are fighting.

I once used that potential confusion about the identity of armies to my advantage. I'd had a number of battles against a troublesome neighbour, with us each in turn marching unsuccessfully on the other's capital, and was now one village away from his capital. The combined army of my two generals was no match for the troops he was sending to meet me and it looked like my only options were to retreat or face defeat. My capital was too far away to send reinforcements in time. Not that I had any to send: the journey to his capital had depleted my army so much that I didn't even have enough troops to spare to raise the negative trade I'd accumulated on the way and so I couldn't afford to take turns on the grid without the risk of desertions. Things weren't looking good.

Then I had a cunning plan. I wasn't at all sure it would work but didn't have anything to lose in trying. I set both my generals to defend their location but didn't station any troops there, then I waited patiently for his general to arrive. When his general was less than 30 seconds away, I put the plan into action: I took both my generals off defence, transferred some troops from each to the location and then put one of them back on defence again. The other general I sent towards my opponent's now undefended capital. As we had declared each other enemies, battle started the moment my opponent's general arrived. He found himself fighting two armies, as he'd expected, but not the same two that he'd been expecting. Instead of fighting both generals, he was fighting one general and the stationed troops. My hope was that he wouldn't notice the switch straightaway and, while he was distracted watching the battle unfold, my other general would have a head start on his capital. I'd been expecting a few minutes head start at most, meaning that he'd probably have time to raise an army at his capital large enough to hold me off until his general returned, but it was a full hour before he realised what I'd done and pulled his general out of the battle to chase after me! By then I was almost at his city and he was unable to catch me or raise an army to defend it in time.

I'm still amazed that my ruse worked as well as it did, although it didn't benefit me much in the end. My newly-conquered follower died shortly afterwards whilst plotting revolt and a revenge attack, and I never got so much as a gold piece out of him in tax. Instead our war left me with a financial crisis and numerous desertions to deal with. Ah well, it was fun anyway.
