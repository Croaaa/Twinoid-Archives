Davout
They're right -- read the FAQ, and also the in-game Help section. You should also take the time to read the multi-age Ask Questions Here thread in this forum.

To answer a few questions up front (Scorpian, just kick me if you wanna get a word in edgewise):

The villages you conquer will originally have a negative trade balance, until the troops you leave there "Pacify" the local brigands (done automatically, over a few turns). The number of troops you need to leave increase as the distance from your capital increase: for villages one intersection away, 5-6 troops will work fine, then increase it by four or five every length of your intersections. You'll also find, the more troops you leave, the higher the trade will increase, until it maxes out around double the number of troops.

The cotton field needs to be conquered before you can collect their resources (same with metal and horse). make sure you ave enough troops to spare to garrison that location, ro that negative balance will be an albatross around your neck (and I speak from experience!).

You're better off capturing as many adjacent savage villages as you can, and man them properly. Keep about a third of your peasants as farmers, with the rest as traders, builders and workers. Work to increase your capital to level 2, so that you can increase your farm to level 2 also, and your barracks at level 2 will allow you to start hiring recruiters, who can help you get more soldiers.

As far as getting more food in your 8x8 resource matrix, the food, wood and hammers appear to arrive randomly, sometimes lots of one kind, some time only a trickle. More gold means more swords appear, more food means more peasants appear (when your on-hand food is at a number equal to the square of your population). Free turns and hammers also appear to arrive randomly.

And no, your troops don't eat food, just gold. Lost and lots of gold...

Good hunting!

I should also say, you get the best results from leaving soldiers equal to double the number of the initial trade imbalance. So, if it's -4, leave 8 troops, if it's -9, leave 18.

Of course, that's tough to start with, which is why you need to hit the nearby ones first.

The other thing to take into account is that your trade imbalance will include the troops you have outside of the barracks in your capital, and any troops over and above the reputation level of your general.

For example: if your barracks allows you to have 5 troops rent-free, when you pull them out and attack a village and capture it, and discover it has a -4 trade imbalance, your total trade imbalance is -9. This doesn't include the one soldier you always have to have with your general, of course.

It gets more complex when you have a general with a higher rep. I have a general, Marshal Lannes, with a rep of 7, which means he carries 6 additional troops for free. When Lannes takes six troops out of my 10-troop garrison in my level 2 barracks capital (which can keep 10 w/o charge), there's no change in cost (since Lannes can keep seven soldiers without charge). But when I capture a village with a -4 trade imbalance, and take six troops out of Lannes' entourage to garrison, all of a sudden, that imbalance jumps to -10 (including the 6 soldiers that are no longer protected by Lannes).

What this means is, until you have a gargantuan gold reserve (and I mean over 500 gold), then taking more than 2 villages at a distance of 3 or more intersections at the same time, is very tough indeed. It means, assuming both are -9, and that you'll need 18 troops at each one to quickly pacify them, your trade imbalance will jump an astounding -54 when you capture them and garrison them ( (18 x 2) + (9 x 2) = 36 = 18 ). And that's per turn, while it takes multiple turns to get the trade imbalance down to a balanced level.

Of course, there's a trick here: assuming you do intend to pacify the villages with a proper number of troops, simply make sure you don't take any "turns" (on the grid, that is) WHILE your general(s) are traveling with their large armies. That way, at least you're not paying for them until they get to the new location, garrison the newly taken villages, and can start bringing the trade imbalance down.

If you don't, and you take say six turns while two generals with a reputation of 1 are traveling for three hours to your borders with 18 troops each (above the 1 troop that'll stay with the general), you've just spent (18 x 2 x 6) 216 gold that you could have saved.

That make sense?

Also, it appears that the more troops you can spare for garrison duty, the higher the amount of trade you'll eventually get, to the tune of almost double the size of the garrison.

Good hunting!
